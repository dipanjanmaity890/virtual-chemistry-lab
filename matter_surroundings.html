Matter in Our Surroundings

Physical nature of matter, states of matter, interconversion, evaporation, and the kinetic theory of particles.

What is Matter?

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space (volume). All matter is made of tiny particles that are in constant motion.

  • Particles are in constant random motion — faster at higher temperature (kinetic energy increases with temperature).
  • Particles attract each other — intermolecular forces hold them together. Stronger forces = more ordered arrangement.
  • Particles have spaces between them — explains why gases are compressible but solids and liquids are not.
Key Experiment — Diffusion: When a crystal of KMnO4 is added to water, the purple colour gradually spreads throughout — demonstrating that particles are in constant random motion. Similarly, the smell of perfume spreads across a room.

Three States of Matter

Solid

Definite shape and volume. Particles are tightly packed in fixed positions with very strong intermolecular forces. Very low kinetic energy. Examples: ice, iron, wood, chalk.

Liquid

Definite volume but no fixed shape (takes the shape of the container). Particles can slide past one another. Moderate intermolecular forces. Examples: water, mercury, alcohol.

Gas

No definite shape or volume. Particles move freely in all directions with high speed. Negligible intermolecular force. Very high kinetic energy. Examples: steam, air, oxygen.

Plasma — fourth state of matter found in stars and lightning. Super-energized ionized gas where electrons separate from atoms.

Interconversion of States

By changing temperature and pressure, matter converts between states. Heat energy is involved in every change:

  • Melting (Fusion): Solid to Liquid. Melting Point of Ice = 0 degrees C (273 K). Heat absorbed.
  • Boiling (Vaporisation): Liquid to Gas. Boiling Point of Water = 100 degrees C (373 K). Heat absorbed.
  • Sublimation: Solid directly to Gas without passing through liquid state. Examples: Dry Ice (CO2), Ammonium Chloride, Iodine, Camphor.
  • Condensation: Gas to Liquid. Heat energy released to surroundings.
  • Freezing (Solidification): Liquid to Solid. Heat energy released.
Latent Heat of Fusion of Ice = 334 J/g (absorbed during melting at 0 degrees C)
Latent Heat of Vaporisation of Water = 2260 J/g (absorbed during boiling at 100 degrees C)

Evaporation and Cooling Effect

Evaporation is a surface phenomenon where high-energy liquid molecules escape into the vapour phase at temperatures below the boiling point. It is a slow process unlike boiling which is a bulk phenomenon.

Factors Increasing Evaporation Rate

  • Higher temperature — more molecules gain energy to escape
  • Larger surface area — more molecules at surface
  • Lower humidity — maintains concentration gradient
  • Higher wind speed — removes vapour, maintains gradient

Cooling Effect of Evaporation

During evaporation, high-energy molecules escape the liquid. The remaining particles have lower average kinetic energy — temperature drops.

Biological applications: Sweating cools the human body. Earthen pots (matka) keep water cool through evaporation through pores. Refrigerators use evaporation of refrigerant.
