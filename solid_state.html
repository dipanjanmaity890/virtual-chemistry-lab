Crystalline vs Amorphous Solids

The Solid State

Crystalline Solids

Possess sharp, regular geometry with **long-range order** of particles. True solids.

• **Melting Point:** Sharp and characteristic.

• **Cleavage:** Splitting yields smooth plane surfaces.

• **Nature:** Anisotropic (physical properties vary along different directions).

• Examples: Quartz, NaCl, Diamond.

Amorphous Solids

Irregular particle arrangements with **short-range order** only. Supercooled liquids.

• **Melting Point:** Soften gradually over a temp range.

• **Cleavage:** Splitting yields irregular surfaces.

• **Nature:** Isotropic (same properties in all directions).

• Examples: Glass, Rubber, Plastics.

Crystal Lattice & Unit Cell

The Solid State

Crystal Lattice: A regular 3D arrangement of constituent particles (represented as points) in space.

Unit Cell: The smallest portion of a crystal lattice which, when repeated in different directions, generates the entire lattice.

  • Defined by 6 parameters: axial lengths (a, b, c) and axial angles (α, β, γ).
  • There are **7 crystal systems** and **14 Bravais lattices**.

Cubic Unit Cell Types

1. Primitive / Simple Cubic (SCC): Atoms at corners only.

2. Body-Centred Cubic (BCC): Atoms at corners + 1 at the center of body.

3. Face-Centred Cubic (FCC): Atoms at corners + 1 at the center of each face.

Calculation of Atoms per Unit Cell

The Solid State

Simple Cubic (SCC)

• 8 corner atoms.

• Contribution: 1/8 per corner.

Z = 8 · (1/8) = 1

Body-Centred (BCC)

• 8 corner atoms (1/8 contribution).

• 1 body center (1 contribution).

Z = 8·(1/8) + 1 = 2

Face-Centred (FCC)

• 8 corner atoms (1/8 contribution).

• 6 face centers (1/2 contribution).

Z = 8·(1/8)+6·(1/2) = 4

Packing Efficiency

The Solid State

Percentage of total space filled by particles. Calculated as volume of spheres divided by total volume of unit cell.

P.E. = (Z · 4/3 π r³) / a³ · 100%
Packing Efficiency Formula

Cubic Systems Summary

• Simple Cubic: a = 2r. **P.E. = 52.4%**

• Body-Centred: a = 4r / √3. **P.E. = 68.0%**

• Face-Centred / CCP: a = 2√2 r. **P.E. = 74.0%**

FCC/CCP represents the closest, most efficient packing possible for spheres.

Density of Crystals

The Solid State

Knowing the unit cell dimensions (edge length a) and composition (Z, Molecular weight M), we can calculate the density of the crystalline solid.

d = (Z · M) / (a³ · N_A)
Density Formula (N_A = Avogadro's Constant)

Units Check

• Z: atoms/cell.

• M: g/mol.

• a: cm (usually given in pm, convert: 1 pm = 10⁻¹⁰ cm).

• N_A: 6.022 × 10²³ mol⁻¹.

• d: g/cm³.

Crystal Defects

The Solid State

Schottky Defect

Equal numbers of **cations and anions are missing** from their normal lattice sites to maintain electrical neutrality.

• Decreases density of solid.

• Shown by highly ionic compounds with similar ion sizes (e.g., NaCl, KCl, CsCl, AgBr).

Frenkel Defect

An ion (usually smaller cation) is **displaced to an interstitial site** in the crystal lattice.

• Density remains unchanged.

• Shown by compounds with large size differences (e.g. ZnS, AgCl, AgI, AgBr).

Note: AgBr exhibits BOTH Schottky and Frenkel defects.
