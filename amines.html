Classification & pyramidal Orbital Structure

Amines

Amines are organic derivatives of ammonia (\(NH_3\)) obtained by replacing one, two, or three hydrogen atoms with alkyl or aryl groups.

Classification

• **Primary (1°):** One hydrogen replaced (e.g. \(R-NH_2\)).

• **Secondary (2°):** Two hydrogens replaced (e.g. \(R_2NH\)).

• **Tertiary (3°):** All three hydrogens replaced (e.g. \(R_3N\)).

Note: Unlike alcohols where class depends on carbon, amine classification depends on the number of groups bound directly to **Nitrogen**.

Pyramidal Geometry

• Nitrogen in amines is **\(sp^3\)-hybridized**.

• Three orbitals form \(\sigma\) bonds with carbon/hydrogen, while the fourth orbital contains a **lone pair** of electrons.

• The geometry is **pyramidal**. Due to lone pair-bonding pair repulsion, the bond angle is compressed to **108°** (less than tetrahedral 109.5°).

Preparation of Amines: Part I

Amines

1. Reduction of Nitro Compounds

• Nitrobenzene is reduced to aniline using metals in acidic medium (\(Sn/HCl\) or \(Fe/HCl\)).

• **Industrial Preference:** Reduction with **\(Fe + HCl\)** is preferred because \(FeCl_2\) formed gets hydrolyzed to release \(HCl\), requiring only a small initiator amount of hydrochloric acid.

2. Ammonolysis of Alkyl Halides

• Nucleophilic substitution where ammonia acts as the nucleophile: \(H_3N + R-X \rightarrow R-NH_3^+ X^- \xrightarrow{NaOH} R-NH_2\).

• **Limitation:** The product primary amine is also nucleophilic and continues to react with alkyl halide, yielding a mixture of \(1^\circ, 2^\circ, 3^\circ\) amines and quaternary ammonium salts. Excess \(NH_3\) favors primary amine formation.

Preparation of Amines: Part II

Amines

1. Gabriel Phthalimide Synthesis

• Phthalimide reacts with KOH to form potassium phthalimide, which undergoes SN2 alkylation with an alkyl halide, followed by alkaline hydrolysis to yield a **pure primary aliphatic amine**:

Pot. Phthalimide + R-X ➔ N-Alkylphthalimide ➔ R-NH₂

**Limitation:** Aryl halides do not undergo SN2 substitution with phthalimide anion. Thus, **Aniline cannot be prepared by this method**.

2. Hofmann Bromamide Degradation

• Treating an amide with bromine in aqueous or ethanolic NaOH degrades the amide carbonyl, yielding a primary amine with **one less carbon**:

R-CONH₂ + Br₂ + 4NaOH ➔ R-NH₂ + Na₂CO₃ + 2NaBr + 2H₂O

Physical Properties

Amines

Boiling Points

Amines form intermolecular hydrogen bonds (except tertiary). Boiling points follow the trend: **Primary > Secondary > Tertiary**.

Hydrogen bonding Limits

Tertiary amines have no hydrogens bonded to nitrogen; they cannot form intermolecular hydrogen bonds and have lower boiling points similar to hydrocarbons.

Water Solubility

Lower aliphatic amines are highly soluble in water because they form H-bonds with H₂O molecules. Solubility decreases as the hydrophobic alkyl chain grows.

Basic Strength of Aliphatic Amines

Amines

Amines are basic due to the presence of a **lone pair on nitrogen**.

**Aliphatic amines are stronger bases than ammonia** because electron-donating alkyl groups (+I effect) increase electron density on nitrogen, making the lone pair more available for protonation and stabilizing the alkylammonium cation.

Gas Phase Basicity Order: R₃N > R₂NH > RNH₂ > NH₃

The Aqueous Phase Anomaly

In water, basicity does not follow the gas phase order. Instead, it is determined by three competing effects:

1. **Inductive Effect (+I):** Stabilizes cation in order \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ\).
2. **Solvation Effect:** H-bonding with water stabilizes cation in order \(1^\circ > 2^\circ > 3^\circ\).
3. **Steric Hindrance:** Crowding blocks protonation in order \(1^\circ > 2^\circ > 3^\circ\).

Aqueous phase Basicity Orders

Amines

Due to the interplay of inductive, solvation, and steric factors, the overall basic strength order in aqueous medium is:

Methyl (-CH₃) Substituted Amines

(CH₃)₂NH > CH₃NH₂ > (CH₃)₃N > NH₃

**Basicity Order:** Secondary (2°) > Primary (1°) > Tertiary (3°) > Ammonia

Ethyl (-C₂H₅) Substituted Amines

(C₂H₅)₂NH > (C₂H₅)₃N > C₂H₅NH₂ > NH₃

**Basicity Order:** Secondary (2°) > Tertiary (3°) > Primary (1°) > Ammonia

Aniline vs Ammonia Basicity

Amines

**Aniline is a much weaker base than ammonia and aliphatic amines** (\(pK_b\) of aniline is 9.4, vs methylamine 3.3).

**Reason:** In aniline, the nitrogen lone pair is in conjugation with the \(\pi\) electron cloud of the benzene ring, delocalizing into the ring via resonance. Thus, the lone pair is less available for protonation.

Anilinium Cation Instability

• Protonation of aniline forms the **Anilinium ion** (\(C_6H_5NH_3^+\)).

• Aniline has **five resonance structures**, whereas the anilinium ion has only **two resonance structures** (since there is no lone pair on nitrogen to delocalize into the ring).

Hence, aniline is much more stable than the anilinium ion, shifting the equilibrium to the left (weaker base).

Carbylamine & Hinsberg Test

Amines

Carbylamine Reaction

• Diagnostic test **only for primary (\(1^\circ\)) amines** (aliphatic or aromatic).

• Heating with chloroform (\(CHCl_3\)) and alcoholic KOH yields extremely foul-smelling **isocyanides (carbylamines)**:

R-NH₂ + CHCl₃ + 3KOH ➔ R-NC + 3KCl + 3H₂O

Secondary and tertiary amines do not show this reaction.

Hinsberg Test Distinction

Reacting amines with **Hinsberg Reagent** (benzenesulfonyl chloride, \(C_6H_5SO_2Cl\)):

• **Primary (1°):** Forms sulfonamide containing acidic hydrogen on N, which is **soluble in alkali**.
• **Secondary (2°):** Forms sulfonamide lacking acidic hydrogen, which is **insoluble in alkali**.
• **Tertiary (3°):** Does not react at all.

Diazonium Salts: Preparation & Synthesis

Amines

Preparation: Diazotisation

Prepared by reacting aniline with nitrous acid (produced in situ from \(NaNO_2\) and \(HCl\)) at low temperatures (**273-278 K**):

C₆H₅NH₂ + NaNO₂ + 2HCl ➔ [0-5°C] ➔ C₆H₅N₂⁺Cl⁻ + NaCl + 2H₂O

Higher temperatures decompose the salt to form phenol.

Synthetic Reactions

• **Sandmeyer Reaction:** Diazonium group replaced by Cl, Br, or CN using cuprous salts (\(Cu_2Cl_2, Cu_2Br_2, Cu_2(CN)_2\)).

• **Gattermann Reaction:** Halogen insertion using copper powder and halogen acid (\(Cu/HCl\)).

• **Coupling Reactions:** Reacting diazonium salts with phenol or aniline yields colored **azo dyes** (e.g. p-hydroxyazobenzene - orange dye).
