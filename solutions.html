Types of Solutions & Characteristics

Solutions

A **solution** is a homogeneous mixture of two or more chemically non-reacting substances whose composition can be varied within certain limits. In a binary solution, the component present in larger quantity is the **solvent**, and the other is the **solute**.

Gaseous Solutions

• **Gas in Gas:** Air (O₂ + N₂ mixture).
• **Liquid in Gas:** Chloroform vapours mixed with nitrogen gas; humidity.
• **Solid in Gas:** Camphor in nitrogen gas; smoke.

Liquid Solutions

• **Gas in Liquid:** Oxygen dissolved in water; aerated drinks (CO₂ in H₂O).
• **Liquid in Liquid:** Ethanol dissolved in water.
• **Solid in Liquid:** Glucose, salt, or sugar dissolved in water.

Solid Solutions

• **Gas in Solid:** Hydrogen gas adsorbed on palladium metal surface.
• **Liquid in Solid:** Amalgam of mercury with sodium metal.
• **Solid in Solid:** Copper dissolved in gold (alloys like Brass, Bronze).

Concentration Expression Methods

Solutions
  • Molarity (M): \(M = \frac{\text{Moles of solute (n)}}{\text{Volume of solution in Liters } (V_L)}\). Unit: mol/L. **Temperature dependent** because volume changes with temperature.
  • Molality (m): \(m = \frac{\text{Moles of solute (n)}}{\text{Mass of solvent in kg } (W_A)}\). Unit: mol/kg. **Temperature independent** as mass does not vary with temperature.
  • Mole Fraction (x): Ratio of moles of one component to total moles: \(x_A = \frac{n_A}{n_A + n_B}\). Note: \(x_A + x_B = 1\). Dimensionless.
  • Parts per Million (ppm): Used for trace solutes: \(\text{ppm} = \frac{\text{Mass of component}}{\text{Total mass of solution}} \times 10^6\).

Solubility of Gases in Liquids: Henry's Law

Solutions

**Henry's Law:** At a constant temperature, the solubility of a gas in a liquid is directly proportional to the partial pressure of the gas present above the surface of the liquid or solution.

p = K_H · x
p = partial pressure, x = mole fraction, K_H = Henry's law constant
  • Different gases have different \(K_H\) values in the same solvent.
  • Higher \(K_H\) value at a given pressure means **lower solubility** of the gas.
  • \(K_H\) increases with temperature; hence gases are **more soluble in cold liquids** than warm.

Key Industrial Applications

1. **Carbonated Beverages:** Soft drink and soda bottles are sealed under high pressure of CO₂ to maximize solubility in the liquid.

2. **Scuba Diving:** Divers breathe air diluted with Helium (11.7% He, 56.2% N₂, 32.1% O₂) to avoid high dissolved N₂ in blood, preventing painful **bends** on ascent.

3. **Anoxia in Climbers:** At high altitudes, low partial pressure of oxygen leads to low blood oxygen levels, causing weakness and mental confusion (Anoxia).

Vapour Pressure of Liquid-Liquid Solutions

Solutions

**Vapour Pressure:** The pressure exerted by vapours in equilibrium with the liquid phase at a specific temperature.

**Raoult's Law:** For a solution of volatile liquids, the partial vapour pressure of each component of the solution is directly proportional to its mole fraction in the solution.

p_A = p°_A · x_A & p_B = p°_B · x_B
Partial pressures where p° represents pure vapour pressure

Total Pressure (Dalton's Law)

The total pressure \(P_{\text{total}}\) over the solution is the sum of the partial pressures:

P_total = p_A + p_B = p°_A·x_A + p°_B·x_B

Using \(x_A = 1 - x_B\):

P_total = p°_A + (p°_B - p°_A)·x_B

Ideal vs Non-Ideal Solutions

Solutions

Ideal Solutions

• Obeys Raoult's law over the entire range of concentration.

• **Molecular Forces:** A-B intermolecular attractive forces are equal to A-A and B-B forces.

• **Thermodynamics:** \(\Delta H_{\text{mixing}} = 0\), \(\Delta V_{\text{mixing}} = 0\).

• Examples: n-Hexane + n-Heptane; Bromoethane + Chloroethane; Benzene + Toluene.

Non-Ideal Solutions

• Do not obey Raoult's law. Vapour pressure is either higher or lower than predicted.

• **Molecular Forces:** A-B attractive forces are different from A-A and B-B forces.

• **Thermodynamics:** \(\Delta H_{\text{mixing}} \neq 0\), \(\Delta V_{\text{mixing}} \neq 0\).

• Examples: Ethanol + Acetone; Phenol + Aniline; Chloroform + Acetone.

Deviations from Raoult's Law & Azeotropes

Solutions

Positive vs Negative Deviation

• **Positive Deviation:** A-B interactions are weaker than A-A/B-B. Vapour pressure is higher. \(\Delta H_{\text{mix}} > 0\) (endothermic), \(\Delta V_{\text{mix}} > 0\). (e.g., Ethanol + Acetone).

• **Negative Deviation:** A-B interactions are stronger than A-A/B-B (due to H-bonding or dipole attractions). Vapour pressure is lower. \(\Delta H_{\text{mix}} < 0\) (exothermic), \(\Delta V_{\text{mix}} < 0\). (e.g., Chloroform + Acetone).

Azeotropes (Constant Boiling Mixtures)

Binary mixtures having the same composition in liquid and vapour phase and boil at a constant temperature. Fractional distillation cannot separate them.

• **Minimum Boiling Azeotrope:** Show large positive deviation. boils at temp lower than both components (e.g., 95% ethanol + 5% water by volume).

• **Maximum Boiling Azeotrope:** Show large negative deviation. boils at temp higher than both (e.g., 68% nitric acid + 32% water by mass).

Colligative Properties: RLVP & Boiling Elevation

Solutions

Colligative properties depend solely on the **number of solute particles** (concentration) relative to total particles, regardless of their nature.

1. Relative Lowering of Vapour Pressure

Adding a non-volatile solute reduces the vapour pressure of the solvent. The relative lowering is equal to the mole fraction of the solute:

(p°_1 - p_1) / p°_1 = x_2 = (w_2 / M_2) / (w_1 / M_1)

Enables determination of solute molar mass \(M_2\).

2. Elevation of Boiling Point (\(\Delta T_b\))

Solute particles decrease vapour pressure, requiring a higher temperature for vapour pressure to equal atmospheric pressure (boiling):

ΔT_b = T_b - T°_b = K_b · m

\(K_b\) is the **Molal Boiling Point Elevation Constant** (Ebullioscopic Constant), unit: K kg/mol.

Colligative Properties: Freezing Depression & Osmosis

Solutions

3. Depression of Freezing Point (\(\Delta T_f\))

A non-volatile solute prevents solvent molecules from arranging into an orderly solid lattice easily, lowering the freezing point:

ΔT_f = T°_f - T_f = K_f · m

\(K_f\) is the **Molal Freezing Point Depression Constant** (Cryoscopic Constant), unit: K kg/mol. Used in ethylene glycol anti-freeze.

4. Osmotic Pressure (\(\pi\))

The hydrostatic pressure that stops net flow of solvent molecules through a semi-permeable membrane from pure solvent to solution:

π = C · R · T = (n_B / V) · R · T

Highly preferred for determining macromolecule (protein, polymer) molar masses because measurements occur at room temperature.

Abnormal Molar Masses & Van 't Hoff Factor

Solutions

When solute molecules dissociate or associate in a solvent, the total number of particles changes, causing observed colligative properties to differ from theoretical ones.

i = Observed Colligative Property / Calculated Colligative Property
Van 't Hoff Factor (i)
i = Normal Molar Mass / Abnormal Molar Mass

Van 't Hoff Rules & Formulas

• **Dissociation:** (e.g., \(NaCl \rightarrow Na^+ + Cl^-\)). Number of particles increases. \(i > 1\). Degree of dissociation: \(\alpha = \frac{i - 1}{n - 1}\).

• **Association:** (e.g., \(2CH_3COOH \rightarrow (CH_3COOH)_2\) dimer). Number of particles decreases. \(i < 1\). Degree of association: \(\alpha = \frac{1 - i}{1 - 1/n}\).

Modified Colligative Equations:
\(\Delta T_b = i K_b m\), \(\Delta T_f = i K_f m\), \(\pi = i C R T\)
