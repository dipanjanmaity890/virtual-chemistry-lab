Classification of Carbohydrates

Biomolecules

Carbohydrates are defined as optically active polyhydroxy aldehydes or ketones, or compounds which produce them on hydrolysis.

Monosaccharides

Simple sugars that cannot be hydrolyzed further into simpler units (e.g. **Glucose, Fructose, Ribose**). About 20 exist in nature.

Oligo / Polysaccharides

• **Oligosaccharides:** Yield 2-10 monosaccharide units on hydrolysis (e.g. **Sucrose, Maltose**).

• **Polysaccharides:** High molar mass polymers yielding many monosaccharides (e.g. **Starch, Cellulose, Glycogen**). Non-sugars.

Reducing vs Non-Reducing

• **Reducing Sugars:** Have free aldehyde/ketone groups; reduce Fehling's and Tollens' reagents (all monosaccharides, lactose, maltose).

• **Non-Reducing:** Lack free functional groups; bound by glycosidic linkages (e.g., **Sucrose**).

Glucose: Open-Chain Structure Evidence

Biomolecules

Chemical reactions proving the structure of D-glucose (aldohexose):

Straight Chain & Carbonyl

1. **Straight Chain:** Prolonged heating with **HI** yields n-hexane, proving all six carbons are linked in a straight chain:

C₆H₁₂O₆ + HI ➔ [heat] ➔ CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₂-CH₃

2. **Carbonyl Group:** Reacts with **hydroxylamine** to form an oxime, and with **HCN** to form cyanohydrin, proving presence of a carbonyl group (\(C=O\)).

Aldehyde & Hydroxyls

3. **Aldehyde:** Oxidation with mild oxidizing agent **bromine water** yields gluconic acid, proving the carbonyl is an aldehyde (-CHO).

4. **Five Hydroxyl Groups:** Acetylation with **acetic anhydride** yields glucose pentaacetate, proving the presence of five hydroxyl (-OH) groups on different carbons.

5. **Primary Alcohol:** Oxidation with **nitric acid** yields saccharic acid (dicarboxylic acid), proving one -OH is primary.

Glucose: Cyclic Structure & Haworth

Biomolecules

Open-Chain Limitations

Despite having an aldehyde group, glucose fails to:

• Form hydrogen sulfite (\(NaHSO_3\)) addition products.

• Give Schiff's reagent test.

• Glucose pentaacetate does not react with hydroxylamine (proving the aldehyde group is locked/not free).

• Glucose exists in two crystalline forms (\(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)) which undergo **mutarotation**.

Anomers & Ring Structure

• Glucose forms a six-membered cyclic hemiacetal ring between C1 (-CHO) and C5 (-OH).

• **Anomers:** Diastereomers differing only in configuration at C1 (anomeric carbon). \(\alpha\)-D-glucose has -OH down; \(\beta\)-D-glucose has -OH up.

• Haworth structures represent this pyranose ring (similar to pyran).

Disaccharides & Polysaccharides

Biomolecules

Sucrose & Inversion

Monosaccharides linked by **glycosidic linkage** (ether bond). In **Sucrose**, C1 of \(\alpha\)-glucose binds C2 of \(\beta\)-fructose. It is dextrorotatory, but hydrolyzes to a levorotatory mixture (D-glucose + D-fructose), hence called **Invert Sugar**.

Maltose & Lactose

• **Maltose:** Two \(\alpha\)-D-glucose units (C1-C4 link). Reducing sugar.

• **Lactose (Milk Sugar):** \(\beta\)-D-galactose + \(\beta\)-D-glucose (C1-C4 link). Reducing sugar.

Polysaccharides

• **Starch:** Plant storage. Amylose (water-soluble, linear \(\alpha\)-1,4 link) + Amylopectin (insoluble, branched \(\alpha\)-1,6 link).

• **Cellulose:** Plant walls. Linear \(\beta\)-D-glucose polymer. Cannot be digested by humans.

Amino Acids & Zwitterions

Biomolecules

Amino acids contain both amino (-NH₂) and carboxyl (-COOH) functional groups.

In aqueous solution, the acidic -COOH group transfers its proton to the basic -NH₂ group, forming a dipolar ion called a **Zwitterion**:

H₃N⁺-CH(R)-COO⁻
Zwitterion structure of α-amino acid

• Zwitterions are amphoteric, reacting with both acids and bases.

Classification & Isoelectric Point

• **Essential Amino Acids:** Cannot be synthesized by the body; must be taken in diet (e.g. Valine, Leucine).

• **Non-Essential:** Synthesized in the body (e.g. Glycine, Alanine).

• **Isoelectric Point (pI):** The specific pH at which the amino acid carries no net charge and does not migrate in an electric field.

Four levels of Protein Structure

Biomolecules

Primary & Secondary

• **Primary:** The specific linear sequence of amino acids in a polypeptide chain. Any change in sequence alters the protein entirely (e.g. Sickle cell hemoglobin).

• **Secondary:** Spatial folding of backbone stabilized by hydrogen bonding between C=O and N-H. Forms **\(\alpha\)-Helix** (intramolecular H-bonds) or **\(\beta\)-Pleated sheets** (intermolecular H-bonds).

Tertiary & Quaternary

• **Tertiary:** Overall 3D folding of polypeptide chains, stabilizing fibrous (insoluble) or globular (soluble) shapes. Held by disulphide bonds, salt bridges, H-bonds, van der Waals.

• **Quaternary:** Spatial arrangement of multiple polypeptide subunits relative to each other (e.g., Hemoglobin has 4 subunits).

Denaturation of Proteins

Biomolecules

Proteins found in biological systems with unique 3D structures and biological activity are called **native proteins**.

When a native protein is subjected to physical changes (e.g., heating) or chemical changes (e.g., pH shift), hydrogen bonds are disrupted.

As a result, globules unfold and helices uncoil, causing the protein to lose its biological activity. This is called **Denaturation**.

Key Mechanisms & Examples

• During denaturation, secondary and tertiary structures are completely destroyed, while the **primary covalent structure remains intact**.

• **Examples:**

1. **Coagulation of egg white:** Boiling denatures albumin, converting it to an insoluble solid mass.
2. **Curdling of milk:** Lactic acid bacteria shift pH, causing denaturation and precipitation of casein milk proteins.

Nucleic Acids: Nucleotides & Nucleosides

Biomolecules

Nucleic acids are long-chain polymers of nucleotides, responsible for heredity transmission and protein synthesis (DNA & RNA).

Nucleoside = Pentose Sugar + Nitrogenous Base
Nucleotide = Pentose Sugar + Base + Phosphoric Acid
Nucleotides link via phosphodiester bonds at C3'-C5'

Sugar & Base Differences

• **DNA (Deoxyribonucleic Acid):** Contains **\(\beta\)-D-2-deoxyribose** sugar. Bases: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), and **Thymine (T)**.

• **RNA (Ribonucleic Acid):** Contains **\(\beta\)-D-ribose** sugar. Bases: Adenine (A), Guanine (G), Cytosine (C), and **Uracil (U)** (Thymine is replaced by Uracil).

DNA Double Helix Watson-Crick model

Biomolecules

Double Helix Structure

• James Watson and Francis Crick proposed the double helix model for DNA.

• Consists of two polynucleotide strands winding around a common axis in **antiparallel directions**.

• The sugar-phosphate backbones form the outer railings, while hydrophobic nitrogenous bases point inward, stack, and pair via hydrogen bonds.

Complementary Base Pairing

Strands are held together by highly specific hydrogen bonds between purines and pyrimidines:

A = T      C ≡ G

• **Adenine** pairs selectively with **Thymine** via 2 hydrogen bonds.
• **Cytosine** pairs selectively with **Guanine** via 3 hydrogen bonds. This base pairing makes the strands complementary.
