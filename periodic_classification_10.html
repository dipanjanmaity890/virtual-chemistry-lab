Periodic Classification of Elements

History of classification, Mendeleev's and Modern Periodic Tables, and periodic trends in properties.

Early Attempts at Classification

  • Dobereiner's Triads (1817): Groups of 3 elements where the middle element's atomic mass is the arithmetic mean of the other two. E.g., Li(7), Na(23), K(39): (7+39)/2=23. Ca(40), Sr(88), Ba(137). Limitation: Only a few triads existed; could not accommodate all elements.
  • Newlands' Law of Octaves (1866): Every 8th element has properties similar to the 1st (like musical octaves). Worked only for lighter elements up to Ca (Z=20). Limitation: Could not accommodate newly discovered noble gases. Failed beyond Ca.
  • Mendeleev's Periodic Table (1869): Arranged elements in order of increasing atomic mass in rows (periods), elements with similar properties in same columns (groups). Predictively left gaps for undiscovered elements: eka-boron=Sc, eka-aluminium=Ga, eka-silicon=Ge (all confirmed later).
  • Anomalies in Mendeleev's Table: Could not explain isotopes (different masses, same element). Position of hydrogen (placed with Group 1 but also resembles Group 17). Pairs like Co(Z=27) and Ni(Z=28) placed out of atomic mass order.

Modern Periodic Table

Moseley's Law (1913): Properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic number (proton number), not atomic mass.

Periods (Horizontal Rows)

7 periods. Period number = number of electron shells (energy levels).

  • Period 1: 2 elements (H, He)
  • Periods 2 and 3: 8 elements each
  • Periods 4 and 5: 18 elements each
  • Periods 6 and 7: 32 elements each

Groups (Vertical Columns)

18 groups. Group number = valence electrons (for main block elements).

  • Group 1: Alkali Metals (Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs, Fr)
  • Group 2: Alkaline Earth Metals
  • Groups 3-12: Transition Metals
  • Group 17: Halogens (F, Cl, Br, I, At)
  • Group 18: Noble Gases (He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn)

Periodic Trends Across Period 3 (Na to Cl)

Atomic Radius

Decreases across period (Na:186pm to Cl:99pm). More protons added without new shells, so nuclear pull increases, pulling electrons closer.

Increases down group (Li:152pm to Cs:265pm). New shells added each period.

Metallic Character

Decreases across period (Na=metal to Si=metalloid to Cl=non-metal). Increases down group.

Valency

Na(1) Mg(2) Al(3) Si(4) P(3/5) S(2) Cl(1) Ar(0). Increases 1-4 then decreases. Down group: remains same.

Non-Metallic Character

Increases across period (towards F/Cl/O). Decreases down group. F = most electronegative element (3.98).

Ionisation Energy

Increases across period. Decreases down group. Na < Al < Cl across period 3.

Electronic Configuration and Position

Period number = Number of electron shells (outermost shell number)
Group number = Number of valence electrons (for s and p-block)

Na (2,8,1): Period 3, Group 1, Valency 1, Alkali Metal
Cl (2,8,7): Period 3, Group 17, Valency 1, Halogen
Ca (2,8,8,2): Period 4, Group 2, Valency 2, Alkaline Earth Metal
Al (2,8,3): Period 3, Group 13, Valency 3, Metal

Modern periodic table resolved Mendeleev's anomalies: isotopes fit (same Z), noble gases fit (Group 18), and all 118 confirmed elements are accommodated.

Modern Periodic Law & Layout

Henry Moseley stated that atomic number is the fundamental periodic property:

  • Periods: 7 horizontal rows. Valence shell number corresponds to period number.
  • Groups: 18 vertical columns. Elements in a group share valence shell configurations.
  • Trend Rule: Atomic radius decreases from left to right, but increases down a group.

NCERT Solved Numerical: Atomic Trends

Problem: Element X has atomic number 12, Y has atomic number 16. Determine their group, period, and which one has a larger atomic radius.

Analysis: * X (Z=12): Configuration = 2, 8, 2 $\rightarrow$ Group 2, Period 3. * Y (Z=16): Configuration = 2, 8, 6 $\rightarrow$ Group 16, Period 3. * **Size comparison:** Both are in Period 3. Since nuclear charge increases left-to-right, X (Z=12) is larger than Y (Z=16).
