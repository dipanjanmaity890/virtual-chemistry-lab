Classification & Bond Geometry

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

Oxygen-containing functional groups derived by substituting hydrogen in hydrocarbons with hydroxyl (-OH) or alkoxy (-OR) groups.

Classification

• **Alcohols:** Hydroxyl group bonded to \(sp^3\)-hybridized carbon of alkyl group (Primary \(1^\circ\), Secondary \(2^\circ\), Tertiary \(3^\circ\)).

• **Phenols:** Hydroxyl group bonded directly to an \(sp^2\)-hybridized aromatic ring carbon.

• **Ethers:** Oxygen bonded to two alkyl or aryl groups. Symmetrical (simple, e.g. \(C_2H_5-O-C_2H_5\)) or unsymmetrical (mixed).

Structures & C-O-H Angles

• In **Methanol**, C-O-H angle is **108.9°** (slightly less than tetrahedral 109.5° due to lone pair repulsions on oxygen).

• In **Phenol**, C-O-H angle is **109°**. The C-O bond length is shorter (136 pm vs 142 pm in methanol) due to partial double bond character from resonance conjugation and \(sp^2\) hybridization of ring carbon.

Preparation of Alcohols

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

1. From Alkenes

• **Acid Catalyzed Hydration:** Reacts with H₂O in presence of H⁺. Obeys **Markovnikov's rule**. Involves carbocation intermediate (rearrangements possible).

• **Hydroboration-Oxidation:** Reacts with diborane (\(B_2H_6\)) followed by alkaline \(H_2O_2\). Results in net **anti-Markovnikov addition of water** in excellent yields (no carbocation rearrangements):

CH₃-CH=CH₂ ➔ CH₃-CH₂-CH₂-OH

2. Reduction & Grignard Additions

• Reduction of aldehydes (gives \(1^\circ\)) and ketones (gives \(2^\circ\)) using \(NaBH_4\) or \(LiAlH_4\).

• **Grignard Addition:** Grignard reagent (\(RMgX\)) adds to carbonyls followed by hydrolysis:

• Formaldehyde (HCHO) ➔ Primary alcohol.
• Other Aldehydes (RCHO) ➔ Secondary alcohol.
• Ketones (RCOR) ➔ Tertiary alcohol.

Preparation of Phenols

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

Phenol (carbolic acid) was first isolated from coal tar. Industrially, it is prepared from cumene or chloro-benzene.

  • From Diazonium Salts: Hydrolysis of benzene diazonium chloride in warm water: \([C_6H_5N_2^+]Cl^- + H_2O \rightarrow C_6H_5OH + N_2 + HCl\).
  • Dow's Process (from Chlorobenzene): Fusion with NaOH at 623 K and 300 atm, followed by acidification.

Cumene Hydroperoxide Process

Most commercial phenol is prepared by oxidation of **Cumene (isopropylbenzene)** in air to form Cumene Hydroperoxide, which is then cleaved with dilute acid:

Cumene + O₂ ➔ Cumene Hydroperoxide ➔ Phenol + Acetone

This is highly economical because it produces valuable **Acetone** as a stoichiometric byproduct in large quantities.

Physical Properties & Hydrogen Bonding

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

Boiling Points

Alcohols and phenols have **much higher boiling points** than ethers, haloalkanes, or hydrocarbons of comparable molar mass. Reason: Intermolecular hydrogen bonding.

H-Bonding Network

The highly polar O-H bond enables oxygen of one molecule to form strong hydrogen bonds with hydrogen of another, creating a closely associated liquid lattice.

Solubility in Water

Lower alcohols are completely miscible in water due to their ability to form H-bonds with H₂O molecules. Solubility **decreases** as the size of the hydrophobic alkyl group increases.

Alcohol Acidity & Lucas Test

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

Acidity of Alcohols

Alcohols are weakly acidic. The acidity order is:

Primary (1°) > Secondary (2°) > Tertiary (3°)

**Reason:** Electron-donating alkyl groups (+I effect) increase electron density on oxygen, making the O-H bond less polar and destabilizing the resulting alkoxide conjugate base.

Distinction: Lucas Test

Lucas reagent (**conc. HCl + anhydrous ZnCl₂**) reacts with alcohols to form insoluble alkyl chlorides, causing turbidity:

• **Tertiary (3°):** Turbidity appears **immediately** (highly stable carbocation forms fast).
• **Secondary (2°):** Turbidity appears in **5 minutes**.
• **Primary (1°):** Turbidity does not appear at room temperature; requires heating.

Dehydration & Oxidation of Alcohols

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

Dehydration to Alkenes/Ethers

• **To Alkenes:** Heating with conc. \(H_2SO_4\) at 443 K yields alkenes. Ease of dehydration order: \(3^\circ > 2^\circ > 1^\circ\).

• **To Ethers:** Heating ethanol with conc. \(H_2SO_4\) at a lower temperature of **413 K** yields ethoxyethane:

2C₂H₅OH ➔ C₂H₅-O-C₂H₅ + H₂O

Oxidation of Alcohols

• **Primary Alcohols:** Oxidized to aldehydes using **PCC (Pyridinium Chlorochromate)**, or to carboxylic acids using alkaline \(KMnO_4\).

• **Secondary Alcohols:** Oxidized to ketones using \(CrO_3\).

• **Dehydrogenation with Cu:** Vapours passed over hot Cu at 573 K:
• \(1^\circ\) ➔ Aldehydes.
• \(2^\circ\) ➔ Ketones.
• \(3^\circ\) ➔ Alkenes (undergoes dehydration instead!).

Acidity: Phenol vs Alcohol

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

**Phenols are significantly more acidic than alcohols** (\(pK_a\) of phenol is 10, alcohols are 16-18).

**Reason:** Ionization of phenol yields a **Phenoxide ion**. The negative charge on oxygen is delocalized over the aromatic ring via resonance, stabilizing the ion. In contrast, the alkoxide ion negative charge is localized on oxygen and destabilized by +I alkyl groups.

Substituent Effects on Acidity

• **Electron Withdrawing Groups (EWGs):** Groups like \(-NO_2\), \(-CN\) stabilize the phenoxide ion by dispersing negative charge. They **increase acidity** (especially at ortho/para positions).

Picric acid (2,4,6-trinitrophenol) is highly acidic, comparable to mineral acids.

• **Electron Donating Groups (EDGs):** Groups like \(-CH_3\), \(-OCH_3\) destabilize the phenoxide ion, **decreasing acidity**.

Phenol Named Reactions

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

1. Kolbe's Reaction

Phenoxide ion reacts with carbon dioxide (a weak electrophile) followed by acidification to yield **Salicylic Acid** (o-hydroxybenzoic acid):

Phenol ➔ [NaOH] ➔ Sodium Phenoxide ➔ [CO₂, H⁺] ➔ Salicylic Acid

Used to manufacture Aspirin.

2. Reimer-Tiemann Reaction

Phenol treated with chloroform (\(CHCl_3\)) in the presence of sodium hydroxide inserts an aldehyde group (-CHO) at ortho- position, yielding **Salicylaldehyde**:

Phenol ➔ [CHCl₃ + NaOH] ➔ Salicylaldehyde

Intermediate electrophile is **Dichlorocarbene (\(:CCl_2\))**.

Ethers: Williamson Synthesis & Cleavage

Alcohols, Phenols & Ethers

Williamson Ether Synthesis

An SN2 reaction where a sodium alkoxide attacks a **primary alkyl halide** to form an ether:

R-O⁻ Na⁺ + R'-X ➔ R-O-R' + NaX

**Limitation:** If a tertiary (\(3^\circ\)) alkyl halide is used, the alkoxide acts as a strong base instead, resulting in **elimination (alkene)** instead of substitution.

Cleavage of Ethers by HX

Cleavage of C-O bond requires strong acids like HI or HBr at high temperatures: \(R-O-R' + HI \rightarrow R-I + R'-OH\).

• **Regiochemistry:** With mixed alkyl groups, iodide attacks the smaller alkyl group (SN2). However, if one alkyl group is tertiary, iodide attacks it to form a tertiary iodide (SN1 mechanism).

• Reaction of **Anisole** with HI yields phenol and methyl iodide (never iodobenzene, as \(sp^2\) C-O bond is too strong).
