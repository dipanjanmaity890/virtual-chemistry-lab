Class 9 · Unit IV

Structure of the Atom

Sub-atomic particles, atomic models (Thomson, Rutherford, Bohr), electronic configuration, isotopes, and valency.

Sub-atomic Particles

Electron (e-)

Discovered by J.J. Thomson (1897) using cathode ray tube. Charge = -1.6x10^-19 C. Mass = 9.11x10^-31 kg (negligible, ~1/1836 of proton).

Proton (p+)

Discovered by Goldstein (1886) via canal rays. Charge = +1.6x10^-19 C. Mass = 1.67x10^-27 kg (approx 1 u).

Neutron (n0)

Discovered by James Chadwick (1932). No electric charge. Mass approx 1 u (slightly more than proton). Located in nucleus alongside protons.

Atomic Number (Z) = No. of protons (= no. of electrons in neutral atom)
Mass Number (A) = Protons + Neutrons
Neutrons = A - Z

Atomic Models

  • Thomson's Plum Pudding Model (1904): Atom is a uniform sphere of positive charge with electrons embedded in it like raisins in a pudding. Drawback: Could not explain Rutherford's gold foil experiment results.
  • Rutherford's Nuclear Model (1911): Alpha particles fired at gold foil. Most passed through (atom is mostly empty space), some deflected (nucleus is positively charged), very few bounced back (nucleus is tiny and dense). Drawback: Could not explain why electrons don't spiral into the nucleus.
  • Bohr's Shell Model (1913): Electrons revolve in fixed discrete circular energy levels (shells: K, L, M, N). Energy is quantized. Electrons absorb/emit energy only when jumping between shells. Resolved stability issue.

Electronic Configuration

Electrons fill shells starting from the innermost. Maximum electrons per shell = 2n^2 (n = shell number).

K(n=1): max 2 | L(n=2): max 8 | M(n=3): max 18 | N(n=4): max 32
  • H (Z=1): 1 | He (Z=2): 2 | Li (Z=3): 2,1 | C (Z=6): 2,4 (Valency=4)
  • N (Z=7): 2,5 (Valency=3) | O (Z=8): 2,6 (Valency=2) | F (Z=9): 2,7 (Valency=1)
  • Na (Z=11): 2,8,1 (Valency=1) | Mg (Z=12): 2,8,2 (Valency=2) | Al (Z=13): 2,8,3 (Valency=3)
  • Cl (Z=17): 2,8,7 (Valency=1) | Ar (Z=18): 2,8,8 (Valency=0, noble gas) | Ca (Z=20): 2,8,8,2 (Valency=2)

Isotopes, Isobars and Isotones

Isotopes

Same Z, different A. Same element, different neutrons. Same chemical properties. Different physical properties.

H-1 (Protium,0n), H-2 (Deuterium,1n), H-3 (Tritium,2n)
C-12 and C-14 (radiocarbon dating)
U-235 and U-238 (nuclear fuel)

Applications

I-131: thyroid cancer | U-235: nuclear reactor | C-14: archaeological dating | Co-60: cancer treatment

Isobars

Same A, different Z. Different elements, same mass number. Different chemical properties.

Ar-40 (Z=18) and Ca-40 (Z=20)

Valency

Combining capacity determined by valence electrons. Valency = valence electrons (if <=4) or 8-valence electrons (if >4).

Elements in same group have same valency. Noble gases have valency=0.
