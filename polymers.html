Classification of Polymers

Polymers

Source Based

• **Natural:** Starch, cellulose, natural rubber.

• **Semi-synthetic:** Cellulose acetate (rayon), cellulose nitrate.

• **Synthetic:** Polythene, Nylon-6,6, Buna-S.

Structure Based

• **Linear:** High density polythene, PVC. Pack well, high density.

• **Branched:** Low density polythene.

• **Cross-linked:** Bakelite, melamine. Form 3D network structure.

Molecular Forces

• **Elastomers:** Weakest forces (rubber).

• **Fibres:** Strongest H-bonding (Nylon, Polyester).

• **Thermoplastic:** Intermediate (PVC).

• **Thermosetting:** Permanent set on heating (Bakelite).

Types of Polymerisation Reactions

Polymers

1. Addition Polymerisation

Also called **chain-growth polymerisation**.

• Monomers contain double/triple bonds.

• Chain grows by adding monomer units one after another.

• No elimination of small molecules.

• Examples: Polythene, Teflon, Orlon (PAN).

2. Condensation Polymerisation

Also called **step-growth polymerisation**.

• Involves repetitive condensation between bi-functional or tri-functional monomers.

• Accompanied by **elimination of small molecules** like H₂O, HCl, NH₃, or alcohol.

• Examples: Terylene, Nylon-6,6, Bakelite.

Important Addition Polymers

Polymers

Polythene

• Low Density Polythene (LDPE): Formed at high pressure (1000-2000 atm) with oxygen initiator. Highly branched, chemically inert, flexible (e.g. squeeze bottles).

• High Density Polythene (HDPE): Formed at low pressure using **Ziegler-Natta catalyst**. Linear, high density, tough (e.g. buckets, pipes).

Teflon & PAN

• Teflon (PTFE): Prepared by heating tetrafluoroethene with persulfate catalyst. Chemically inert, heat resistant (used for non-stick pan coatings).

Important Condensation Polymers

Polymers

Nylons (Polyamides)

• Nylon-6,6: Condensation of Hexamethylenediamine and Adipic acid. High tensile strength, used in bristles, sheets.

• Nylon-6: Prepared by heating Caprolactam with water. Used in ropes, tyre cords.

Polyesters & Bakelite

• Terylene / Dacron: Condensation of ethylene glycol and terephthalic acid. Crease-resistant.

• Bakelite: Phenol-formaldehyde crosslinked polymer. Thermosetting, hard, scratch-resistant (used in electrical switches, plugs).

Rubbers (Natural & Synthetic)

Polymers

Natural Rubber: Linear polymer of **isoprene** (cis-1,4-polyisoprene). Sticky, low tensile strength.

Vulcanisation: Heating raw rubber with **sulfur** (3-5%) and additives at 373-415 K. Creates crosslinked sulfur bridges, making it tough, non-sticky, and highly elastic.

Synthetic Rubbers

• Neoprene: Polymer of chloroprene. Resistant to oils and solvents.

• Buna-S: Copolymer of 1,3-butadiene and styrene. Highly durable, used in vehicle tyres.

• Buna-N: Copolymer of 1,3-butadiene and acrylonitrile. Resistant to petrol.

Biodegradable Polymers

Polymers

Most synthetic polymers are non-biodegradable and accumulate as waste, causing environmental hazards. To address this, biodegradable synthetic polymers have been developed:

  • Contain linkages (like esters or amides) that can be easily broken down by microbial enzymes.

Major Examples

• PHBV: Poly-β-hydroxybutyrate-co-β-hydroxyvalerate. Prepared from 3-hydroxybutanoic acid and 3-hydroxypentanoic acid. Used in specialty packaging and orthopedic devices.

• Nylon 2-Nylon 6: Alternating polyamide copolymer of glycine and aminocaproic acid.
