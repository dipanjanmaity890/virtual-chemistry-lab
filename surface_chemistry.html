Adsorption vs Absorption

Surface Chemistry

Adsorption

A **surface phenomenon** where substance concentration is higher at the boundary than in the bulk.

• **Adsorbate:** Substance accumulating at the surface.

• **Adsorbent:** Solid surface where adsorption takes place.

• Examples: Silica gel adsorbs water vapour.

Absorption

A **bulk phenomenon** where substance is uniformly distributed throughout the body of the solid/liquid.

• Examples: Sponge absorbs water, anhydrous CaCl₂ absorbs water vapour.

Sorption represents when BOTH processes happen simultaneously.

Physisorption vs Chemisorption

Surface Chemistry

Physical Adsorption (Physisorption)

• Held by weak **Van der Waals forces**.

• Not specific. Reversible.

• Low enthalpy of adsorption (20-40 kJ/mol).

• Favoured by low temperature.

• Forms multi-molecular layers.

Chemical Adsorption (Chemisorption)

• Held by strong **chemical bonds** (covalent/ionic).

• Highly specific. Irreversible.

• High enthalpy of adsorption (80-240 kJ/mol).

• Favoured by high temperature.

• Forms monomolecular layer.

Freundlich Adsorption Isotherm

Surface Chemistry

Describes the variation in the quantity of gas adsorbed by a unit mass of solid adsorbent with pressure at a constant temperature.

x/m = k · p^(1/n)
Freundlich Isotherm Equation (n > 1)

Logarithmic Form

log(x/m) = log(k) + 1/n log(p)

Plotting log(x/m) vs log(p) gives a straight line with slope 1/n and intercept log(k), validating the isotherm.

Colloidal State

Surface Chemistry

A heterogeneous mixture where particle size ranges between **1 nm and 1000 nm** (dispersed phase in dispersion medium).

Lyophilic Colloids

Liquid loving. Strong affinity between dispersed phase and medium. Reversible. Highly stable, do not coagulate easily (e.g. gum, gelatin, starch).

Lyophobic Colloids

Liquid hating. Little or no affinity. Irreversible. Unstable, coagulate easily upon addition of electrolytes (e.g. metals and their sulfides sols).

Tyndall Effect & Brownian Motion

Surface Chemistry

Tyndall Effect

The scattering of light by colloidal particles, rendering the path of the beam visible. Requires:

1. Particle diameter is not much smaller than wavelength of light.

2. Large difference in refractive indices of dispersed phase and medium.

Brownian Movement

Continuous, random, zig-zag motion of colloidal particles in a sol.

• Caused by unbalanced bombardment of sol particles by molecules of dispersion medium.

• Prevents particles from settling down, contributing to sol stability.

Electrophoresis & Hardy-Schulze

Surface Chemistry

Electrophoresis

The migration of colloidal particles under the influence of an applied electric field. Confirms the presence of electric charge on sol particles.

Particles migrate to opposite electrode and discharge.

Hardy-Schulze Rule

The coagulating power of an electrolyte depends on the valency of the active flocculating ion (ion carrying charge opposite to sol):

For negative sol: Al³⁺ > Ba²⁺ > Na⁺

For positive sol: [Fe(CN)₆]⁴⁻ > PO₄³⁻ > SO₄²⁻ > Cl⁻
