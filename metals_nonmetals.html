Class 10 · Unit III

Metals and Non-Metals

Physical and chemical properties, ionic bonding, reactivity series, extraction of metals, and corrosion.

Physical Properties

Metals

  • Lustrous (shiny, reflect light)
  • Hard and strong (except Na, K: soft; Hg: liquid at room temp)
  • Malleable (can be hammered into sheets)
  • Ductile (can be drawn into wires)
  • Good conductors of heat and electricity (free electrons)
  • High melting and boiling points (except Hg and Ga)
  • Sonorous (ring when struck)

Non-Metals

  • Non-lustrous (dull, except Iodine which is lustrous)
  • Brittle when solid (break/crumble)
  • Non-malleable and non-ductile
  • Poor conductors of heat and electricity (exception: Graphite is a good conductor)
  • Low melting and boiling points (many are gases at room temp)
  • Non-sonorous

Chemical Reactions of Metals

  • With Oxygen: 4Na+O2 gives 2Na2O | 2Mg+O2 gives 2MgO (bright white flame) | 2Cu+O2 gives 2CuO (black on heating)
  • With Water: 2Na+2H2O gives 2NaOH+H2 (violent) | Ca+2H2O gives Ca(OH)2+H2 (vigorous) | 3Fe+4H2O(steam) gives Fe3O4+4H2 (at high temp)
  • With Dilute Acids: Zn+H2SO4 gives ZnSO4+H2 | Fe+2HCl gives FeCl2+H2 | Mg+2HCl gives MgCl2+H2
  • With Salt Solutions: Fe+CuSO4 gives FeSO4+Cu (Fe more reactive, displaces Cu) | Cu+2AgNO3 gives Cu(NO3)2+2Ag
Activity Series (Decreasing Reactivity):
K > Na > Ca > Mg > Al > Zn > Fe > Pb > H > Cu > Hg > Ag > Pt > Au

Ionic Bond Formation

Metals form ionic bonds by transferring electrons to non-metals. Metal becomes cation (+ve), non-metal becomes anion (-ve). Electrostatic attraction holds them.

NaCl Formation

Na (2,8,1) loses 1e- and becomes Na+ (2,8) - noble gas configuration.
Cl (2,8,7) gains 1e- and becomes Cl- (2,8,8) - noble gas configuration.
Na+ + Cl- = NaCl (ionic bond). Melting point: 801 degrees C.

MgCl2 Formation

Mg (2,8,2) loses 2e- and becomes Mg2+ (2,8).
Two Cl each gain 1e- and become 2Cl- (2,8,8).
Mg2+ + 2Cl- = MgCl2. Melting point: 714 degrees C.

Properties of Ionic Compounds

High melting/boiling point. Soluble in water. Conduct electricity when dissolved or molten (not solid).

Corrosion and Prevention

Corrosion

Attack of metals by environment. Rusting of iron: 4Fe+3O2+xH2O gives 2Fe2O3.xH2O (requires both O2 and H2O). Green patina on copper: Cu(OH)2.CuCO3. Tarnishing of silver: Ag2S.

Rusting does NOT occur in dry air or water alone. Both O2 and moisture needed simultaneously.

Corrosion Prevention

  • Painting/Oiling: Physical barrier from air and moisture
  • Galvanisation: Zinc coating on iron (sacrificial protection)
  • Tinning: Tin coating on iron for food cans
  • Electroplating: Chrome/nickel plating on car parts
  • Alloying: Stainless steel = Fe+Cr+Ni (Cr2O3 layer protects)

Metal Activity Series

Metals are arranged in decreasing order of reactivity. Highly active metals displace less active ones:

  • Highly Active: Potassium ($K$), Sodium ($Na$) react violently with cold water.
  • Moderately Active: Iron ($Fe$), Zinc ($Zn$) react only with steam.
  • Least Active: Copper ($Cu$), Gold ($Au$) do not react with water or acids.

Process (Roasting in excess Oxygen): $$2ZnS(s) + 3O_2(g) \xrightarrow{\text{Heat}} 2ZnO(s) + 2SO_2(g)$$ * Note: Roasting converts sulfide ores into oxides, releasing gaseous sulfur dioxide.
