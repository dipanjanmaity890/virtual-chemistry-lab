Acids, Bases and Salts

Properties of acids and bases, pH scale, neutralization, important salts, and applications in daily life.

Acids and Bases

Acids

Produce H+ ions in aqueous solution (Arrhenius). Turn blue litmus red. Sour taste.

  • HCl: Hydrochloric Acid (gastric acid in stomach)
  • H2SO4: Sulphuric Acid (battery acid, king of chemicals)
  • HNO3: Nitric Acid (fertilizer production)
  • CH3COOH: Ethanoic/Acetic Acid (vinegar)
  • HCOOH: Methanoic Acid (ant/bee sting)

Bases

Produce OH- ions in water. Turn red litmus blue. Bitter taste, soapy feel.

  • NaOH: Sodium Hydroxide (caustic soda, soap-making)
  • Ca(OH)2: Calcium Hydroxide (lime water, white wash)
  • NH4OH: Ammonium Hydroxide (cleaning agent)
  • Mg(OH)2: Milk of Magnesia (antacid)
  • Al(OH)3: Aluminium Hydroxide (antacid)

pH Scale and Indicators

pH = -log[H+]. pH scale ranges from 0 (most acidic) to 14 (most basic). pH 7 = neutral.

  • Conc. HCl: pH 0-1 | Lemon/Lime juice: pH 2 | Vinegar: pH 3 | Tomato: pH 4 | Coffee: pH 5
  • Pure distilled water: pH 7 | Blood: pH 7.4 | Sea water: pH 8 | Baking soda: pH 9
  • Soap solution: pH 9-10 | Milk of Magnesia: pH 10-11 | Conc. NaOH: pH 13-14
  • Universal Indicator: Shows pH by changing through multiple colours (red to violet).
  • Phenolphthalein: Colourless in acid, pink/magenta in base. Used in titrations.
  • Methyl Orange: Red in acid, yellow in base.

Neutralisation and Salts

Acid + Base gives Salt + Water. Neutralisation is exothermic.

HCl + NaOH gives NaCl + H2O (common salt, neutral pH 7)
H2SO4 + 2NaOH gives Na2SO4 + 2H2O (sodium sulphate)
  • NaCl (Common Salt, pH 7): Food preservation, making NaOH by electrolysis, Na, Cl2, NaHCO3 from Chlor-alkali process.
  • NaHCO3 (Baking Soda, pH 9-10): Releases CO2 on heating. Used in baking (CO2 makes dough rise), antacid, fire extinguishers.
  • Na2CO3.10H2O (Washing Soda, pH 11-12): Softens hard water (removes Ca2+/Mg2+), used in glass, soap, paper industries.
  • CaOCl2 (Bleaching Powder): Disinfects water supply, bleaches cotton cloth, used in paper mills.
  • CaSO4.0.5H2O (Plaster of Paris): Sets hard by absorbing water: 2CaSO4.0.5H2O + 3H2O gives 2CaSO4.2H2O. Used for casts/moulds.

Acids and Bases in Daily Life

Biological Examples

Bee/Ant Sting: Formic acid (HCOOH) injected. Apply baking soda (base) to neutralize pain.

Indigestion/Acidity: Excess HCl in stomach. Antacid containing Mg(OH)2 or NaHCO3 neutralizes acid.

Tooth Decay: Bacteria produce lactic acid. pH drops below 5.5 degrades enamel (calcium phosphate).

Environmental Examples

Acid Rain: SO2 and NOx react with water to form H2SO4 and HNO3. pH less than 5.6. Damages marble monuments (CaCO3 + H2SO4 gives CaSO4 + H2O + CO2).

Soil pH: Excess acid soil treated with quicklime (CaO) or slaked lime Ca(OH)2. Excess alkaline soil treated with gypsum (CaSO4).

Understanding Acid-Base Strengths

The strength depends on the extent of ionization (splitting into ions):

  • Strong Acids: Split completely in water. Example: $HCl, H_2SO_4$.
  • Weak Acids: Split partially. Example: Citric acid, Acetic acid ($CH_3COOH$).
  • Neutralization: Reaction between acid and base to form salt and water: $$\text{Acid} + \text{Base} \rightarrow \text{Salt} + \text{Water} + \text{Heat}$$

NCERT Solved Numerical: pH Calculation

Formula: Logarithmic acidity scale $$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[H^+]$$

Problem: An aqueous solution has a hydrogen ion concentration $[H^+] = 10^{-3}\text{ M}$. Calculate its pH and identify its nature.

Calculation: $$\text{pH} = -\log_{10}[10^{-3}] = -(-3) = 3$$ * Since the pH value ($3$) is less than $7$, the solution is **highly acidic**.
