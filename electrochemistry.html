Electrochemical & Electrolytic Cells

Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry is the study of production of electricity from energy released during spontaneous chemical reactions and the use of electrical energy to bring about non-spontaneous transformations.

Galvanic / Voltaic Cells

• Converts **chemical energy of a spontaneous redox reaction** into electrical energy.

• **Anode:** Negative electrode (Oxidation: Zn ➔ Zn²⁺ + 2e⁻).

• **Cathode:** Positive electrode (Reduction: Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ ➔ Cu).

• Electrons flow from Anode to Cathode through external circuit.

• Example: Daniel Cell.

Electrolytic Cells

• Uses **external electrical energy** to drive a non-spontaneous chemical reaction.

• **Anode:** Positive electrode (Oxidation occurs here).

• **Cathode:** Negative electrode (Reduction occurs here).

• Electrolyte decomposes upon passing electric current.

• Example: Electrolysis of molten NaCl.

Galvanic Cell EMF & Representation

Electrochemistry

Electrode Potential: Potential difference developed between the metal electrode and its ion solution at equilibrium.

Standard Hydrogen Electrode (SHE): Reference electrode whose standard reduction potential is assigned as **0.00 V** at all temperatures.

Cell Potential (EMF): Difference between reduction potentials of cathode and anode:

E°_cell = E°_cathode - E°_anode
Measured in Volts. Note: E° values must be reduction potentials.

Cell Representation Rules

• Oxidation half-cell on the left, Reduction half-cell on the right.

• Single vertical line represents phase boundary, double vertical line represents salt bridge.

Zn(s) | Zn²⁺(aq, C₁) || Cu²⁺(aq, C₂) | Cu(s)

**Salt Bridge Function:** Maintains electrical neutrality in both half-cells and completes the circuit without mixing solutions.

The Nernst Equation

Electrochemistry

Determines the cell potential at non-standard concentrations and temperatures. For a general electrode reaction: \(M^{n+}(aq) + ne^- \rightarrow M(s)\):

E = E° - (RT / nF) · ln(1 / [M^n+])

For a complete cell reaction at 298 K:

E_cell = E°_cell - (0.0591 / n) · log(Q)
Q = Reaction Quotient = [Product ions] / [Reactant ions]

Equilibrium & Free Energy

• **Equilibrium Constant (\(K_c\)):** At equilibrium, \(E_{\text{cell}} = 0\), so:

log K_c = (n · E°_cell) / 0.0591

• **Gibbs Free Energy change (\(\Delta G\)):** Represents maximum electrical work done by the cell:

ΔG = -n · F · E_cell & ΔG° = -n · F · E°_cell

F = Faraday constant = 96487 C/mol.

Conductance in Electrolytic Solutions

Electrochemistry

Conductivity (κ)

Conductance of 1 cm³ of electrolyte solution. Unit: S/cm. Decreases upon dilution because number of ions per unit volume decreases.

κ = (1 / R) · (l / A)

Molar Conductivity (Λm)

Conducting power of all ions produced by dissolving 1 mole of electrolyte. Unit: S cm²/mol. Increases with dilution.

Λm = (κ × 1000) / C

Strong vs Weak

• **Strong:** Λm increases slowly with dilution due to lower inter-ionic attractions.

• **Weak:** Λm increases steeply at high dilution due to increased degree of dissociation (\(\alpha\)).

Kohlrausch's Law of Independent Migration

Electrochemistry

**Statement:** Limiting molar conductivity of an electrolyte (\(\Lambda^\circ_m\)) can be represented as the sum of the individual limiting molar conductivities of its constituent anions and cations.

Λ°m = ν₊ λ°₊ + ν₋ λ°₋
ν represents stoichiometric coefficients of ions

Key Applications

1. **Weak Electrolyte Molar Conductivity:** Calculate \(\Lambda^\circ_m\) for weak electrolytes using strong electrolyte values:

Λ°(CH₃COOH) = Λ°(CH₃COONa) + Λ°(HCl) - Λ°(NaCl)

2. **Degree of Dissociation (\(\alpha\)):**

α = Λm / Λ°m

3. **Dissociation Constant (\(K_a\)):** \(K_a = \frac{C \alpha^2}{1 - \alpha}\).

Faraday's Laws of Electrolysis

Electrochemistry

First Law

The mass (\(w\)) of a chemical substance deposited or liberated at any electrode during electrolysis is directly proportional to the quantity of electricity (\(Q\)) passed through the electrolyte:

w = Z · Q = Z · I · t

Z = Electrochemical equivalent. I = current in Amperes, t = time in seconds.

Second Law

When the same quantity of electricity is passed through different electrolyte solutions, the masses of substances liberated are proportional to their chemical equivalent weights:

w_1 / w_2 = E_1 / E_2

Equivalent weight \(E = \text{Molar mass} / \text{valence factor}\).

Batteries: Primary & Secondary Cells

Electrochemistry

Primary Cells

Reaction occurs only once; cannot be recharged. (e.g., **Dry Cell** - Zinc container anode, MnO₂/C cathode; **Mercury Cell** - provides steady 1.35 V, used in hearing aids).

Secondary Cells

Can be recharged by passing current in opposite direction. (e.g. **Lead Accumulator** - Lead anode, PbO₂ cathode, 38% H₂SO₄ electrolyte; **Nickel-Cadmium cell**).

Fuel Cells

Convert combustion energy of fuels directly to electricity. **H₂-O₂ Fuel Cell** reactions:
Anode: 2H₂ + 4OH⁻ ➔ 4H₂O + 4e⁻
Cathode: O₂ + 2H₂O + 4e⁻ ➔ 4OH⁻

Corrosion & Rusting of Iron

Electrochemistry

Corrosion is an **electrochemical phenomenon**. At a particular spot on the iron surface, oxidation takes place, acting as an **anode**:

Anode: 2Fe(s) ➔ 2Fe²⁺ + 4e⁻ (E° = -0.44 V)

Released electrons migrate through the metal to another spot, reducing atmospheric oxygen in the presence of H⁺ (from H₂CO₃ in moisture), acting as a **cathode**:

Cathode: O₂ + 4H⁺ + 4e⁻ ➔ 2H₂O (E° = 1.23 V)

Overall Reaction & Prevention

2Fe + O₂ + 4H⁺ ➔ 2Fe²⁺ + 2H₂O (E°_cell = 1.67 V)

Fe²⁺ ions are further oxidized by atmospheric oxygen to form hydrated ferric oxide, **Rust**: \(Fe_2O_3 \cdot xH_2O\).

**Prevention Methods:**

• Barrier protection (painting, oiling).
• Sacrificial protection / **Galvanisation** (coating iron with a more active metal like Zinc).
