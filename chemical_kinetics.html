Rate of a Chemical Reaction

Chemical Kinetics

The speed at which reactants are converted into products. It is defined as the change in concentration of a reactant or a product per unit time.

Average vs Instantaneous Rate

• **Average Rate (\(r_{\text{avg}}\)):** Change in concentration over a finite time interval \(\Delta t\):

r_avg = -Δ[R]/Δt = +Δ[P]/Δt

• **Instantaneous Rate (\(r_{\text{inst}}\)):** Rate at a specific instant of time (when \(\Delta t \rightarrow 0\)):

r_inst = -d[R]/dt = +d[P]/dt

Unit of rate: **mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹** (or atm s⁻¹ for gases).

Stoichiometric Coefficients

For a general reaction: \(aA + bB \rightarrow cC + dD\), rates must be divided by stoichiometry to be equal:

Rate = -1/a d[A]/dt = -1/b d[B]/dt
     = +1/c d[C]/dt = +1/d d[D]/dt

Factors Influencing Reaction Rates

Chemical Kinetics
  • Concentration: Higher concentration of reactants increases the frequency of molecular collisions, speeding up the reaction.
  • Temperature: Raising the temperature increases the kinetic energy of molecules. Generally, the rate constant **doubles** for every 10° rise in temperature.
  • Catalyst: Provides an alternative pathway with a **lower activation energy**, accelerating both forward and backward rates.

Other Crucial Factors

• **Nature of Reactants:** Ionic reactions (e.g. \(Ag^+ + Cl^- \rightarrow AgCl(s)\)) are extremely fast compared to covalent bond-breaking reactions.

• **Surface Area:** Fine powders react faster than large solid chunks (e.g., powdered coal burns instantly compared to coal lumps).

• **Presence of Light:** Photochemical reactions (e.g., photosynthesis, \(H_2 + Cl_2 \xrightarrow{h\nu} 2HCl\)) depend on light intensity.

Rate Law & Order of Reaction

Chemical Kinetics

Rate Law: Mathematical expression representing the reaction rate in terms of molar concentrations of reactants raised to powers determined experimentally (which may or may not equal coefficients).

Rate = k · [A]^x · [B]^y
k = Rate Constant / Specific Reaction Rate

Order of Reaction: The sum of powers (\(x + y\)) of concentrations in the rate law. Can be integer, fractional, or zero.

Units of Rate Constant (k)

Derived using: \([k] = (\text{mol L}^{-1})^{1-n} \cdot s^{-1}\) (where n is order):

• Zero Order (n=0): mol L⁻¹ s⁻¹
• First Order (n=1): s⁻¹
• Second Order (n=2): L mol⁻¹ s⁻¹

**Physical Significance of k:** High rate constant indicates a fast reaction. It is independent of reactant concentration but changes with temperature.

Molecularity vs Order of Reaction

Chemical Kinetics

For elementary reactions (single-step), molecularity is the number of reacting species colliding simultaneously to yield products.

Order of Reaction

• Sum of powers in experimental rate law.

• Applicable to both elementary and complex reactions.

• Can be integer, fraction, or **zero**.

• Determined experimentally only.

Molecularity

• Number of reacting atoms/ions/molecules.

• Applicable **only to elementary reactions** (meaningless for complex ones).

• Always a positive whole number (never zero or fraction).

• Theoretical concept derived from mechanism.

Integrated Rate Law: Zero Order

Chemical Kinetics

For: \(R \rightarrow P\), where rate is independent of concentration: \(-d[R]/dt = k [R]^0 = k\).

Integrating from \(t=0\) to \(t\):

[R] = -k·t + [R]_0
Linear equation. Plotting [R] vs t yields a slope = -k
k = ([R]_0 - [R]) / t

Half-Life (\(t_{1/2}\))

Time required for reactant concentration to reduce to half of its initial value. For zero order, setting \([R] = [R]_0 / 2\):

t_1/2 = [R]_0 / (2k)

Hence, half-life of a zero-order reaction is **directly proportional** to its initial concentration.

Integrated Rate Law: First Order

Chemical Kinetics

For: \(R \rightarrow P\), where rate depends on first power: \(-d[R]/dt = k[R]\). Rearranging and integrating gives \(\ln [R] = -kt + \ln [R]_0\). Converting to base 10 log:

k = (2.303 / t) · log([R]_0 / [R])
Or: [R] = [R]_0 · e^(-kt) (exponential decay)

Half-Life & Graphs

Setting \([R] = [R]_0 / 2\):

t_1/2 = 0.693 / k

The half-life of a first-order reaction is **independent of the initial concentration**.

• Plotting \(\log([R]_0/[R])\) vs \(t\) yields a straight line with slope = \(k / 2.303\).

Pseudo First Order Reactions

Chemical Kinetics

Reactions which are not truly first-order but behave as first-order under specific conditions (e.g., when one reactant is present in **large excess**, causing its concentration to remain practically constant).

Example 1: Acid Hydrolysis of Ester

CH₃COOC₂H₅ + H₂O ➔ CH₃COOH + C₂H₅OH

Since water is the solvent (excess), \(\text{Rate} = k' [\text{Ester}][H_2O] = k [\text{Ester}]\).

Example 2: Inversion of Cane Sugar

C₁₂H₂₂O₁₁ + H₂O ➔ C₆H₁₂O₆ + C₆H₁₂O₆

Hydrolysis of sucrose yields Glucose + Fructose. Again, concentration of water does not change significantly during the reaction.

Temperature Dependence & Arrhenius

Chemical Kinetics

Arrhenius Equation: Expresses rate constant dependency on temperature:

k = A · e^(-E_a / RT)
A = Frequency factor, E_a = Activation energy

Comparing rates at two temperatures \(T_1\) and \(T_2\):

log(k_2/k_1) = E_a/2.303R · (1/T_1 - 1/T_2)

Activation Energy (Ea)

The **minimum excess energy** over ground state that reactants must acquire to form an activated complex/transition state before forming products.

• Exothermic or endothermic reactions both require crossing this energy barrier.

Collision Theory of Reactions

Chemical Kinetics

Assumes reactant molecules are **hard spheres** and reaction occurs when they collide. For a collision to be effective (lead to products), it must satisfy two criteria:

  • Energy Barrier: Colliding molecules must possess minimum kinetic energy called **threshold energy**.
  • Orientation Barrier: Reactants must collide in the proper spatial alignment to allow bond formation.

Mathematical Rate Law

Rate = P · Z_AB · e^(-E_a/RT)

• \(Z_{AB}\): Collision frequency (collisions per second per unit volume).
• \(e^{-E_a/RT}\): Fraction of molecules with kinetic energy \(\ge E_a\).
• \(P\): Orientation/probability factor.
