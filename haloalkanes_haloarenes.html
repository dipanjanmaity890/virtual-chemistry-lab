Classification & C-X Bond Chemistry

Haloalkanes

Alkyl and aryl halides are compounds where hydrogen atoms in alkanes or arenes are replaced by halogens.

Classification Types

• **Allylic Halides:** Halogen bonded to \(sp^3\)-hybridized carbon adjacent to a C=C double bond (e.g. \(CH_2=CH-CH_2X\)).

• **Benzylic Halides:** Halogen bonded to \(sp^3\)-hybridized carbon attached to an aromatic ring.

• **Vinylic Halides:** Halogen bonded directly to an \(sp^2\)-hybridized carbon of double bond.

• **Aryl Halides:** Halogen bonded directly to aromatic ring carbon.

Nature of C-X Bond

• Halogen is more electronegative than carbon, making the **C-X bond highly polar**.

• Carbon carries partial positive (\(\delta^+\)) and halogen carries partial negative (\(\delta^-\)) charge.

• **Bond Strength Trend:** Down the group, size of halogen increases. C-F bond is shortest/strongest, C-I is longest/weakest. Consequently, reactivity increases in the order: **R-F < R-Cl < R-Br < R-I**.

Methods of Preparation

Haloalkanes

1. From Alcohols

• Reacting with \(HX\) in presence of \(ZnCl_2\) (Lucas Reagent), or \(PCl_3\)/\(PCl_5\).

• **Darzens Process:** Best method uses **thionyl chloride (\(SOCl_2\))** because byproducts are gases (\(SO_2\) and \(HCl\)), leaving pure alkyl chloride:

R-OH + SOCl₂ ➔ R-Cl + SO₂↑ + HCl↑

2. Additions & Halogen Exchange

• **Markovnikov addition:** \(HX\) adds to unsymmetrical alkenes; halide goes to more substituted carbon.

• **Finkelstein Reaction (Iodide prep):**

R-Cl + NaI ➔ R-I + NaCl↓ (in dry acetone)

• **Swarts Reaction (Fluoride prep):**

R-Br + AgF ➔ R-F + AgBr↓ (uses AgF, Hg₂F₂ or CoF₂)

Physical Properties

Haloalkanes

Boiling Points

Higher than corresponding hydrocarbons due to stronger polar dipole-dipole attractions. Trend: **R-I > R-Br > R-Cl > R-F** (higher mass = higher London forces).

Branching Effect

For isomeric alkyl halides, **branching decreases the boiling point**. Branching makes the molecule spherical, reducing surface area and decreasing van der Waals attractive forces.

Isomer Melting Points

Among o-, m-, and p-dichlorobenzenes, the **para-isomer has the highest melting point**. Its symmetrical structure fits perfectly into the crystal lattice, requiring more energy to melt.

SN2 Reaction Mechanism

Haloalkanes

SN2 (Substitution Nucleophilic Biomolecular): occurs in a single step with a transition state. The incoming nucleophile attacks from the **backside** (opposite to the leaving group).

Rate = k · [Substrate] · [Nucleophile]
Second-order kinetics. No intermediate.

Walden Inversion: The configurations invert like an umbrella turning inside out in a strong wind.

SN2 Reactivity Factors

• Driven by **steric hindrance**. Smaller substrates allow easier backside attack.

• **Substrate Reactivity Order:**

Methyl > 1° > 2° > 3°

• Tertiary halides are extremely slow in SN2 because bulky groups block nucleophile approach.

SN1 Reaction Mechanism

Haloalkanes

Mechanism Steps

• **Step 1 (Slow/RDS):** Heterolytic cleavage of C-X bond to form a planar **carbocation intermediate** and halide ion.

• **Step 2 (Fast):** Attack of nucleophile from either front or backside on the planar carbocation.

Rate = k · [Substrate]

First-order kinetics. Independent of nucleophile strength/concentration.

SN1 Reactivity Factors

• Driven by **carbocation stability** (polar protic solvents like H₂O, EtOH stabilize carbocations via solvation).

• **Substrate Reactivity Order:**

3° > 2° > 1° > Methyl

• Allylic and benzylic halides show high reactivity towards SN1 due to resonance stabilization of their carbocations.

Stereochemistry of Substitution

Haloalkanes
  • Chirality: Molecules containing an asymmetric carbon (bonded to 4 different groups) are chiral, lacking an internal plane of symmetry.
  • Enantiomers: Non-superimposable mirror images that rotate plane-polarized light in equal and opposite directions.
  • Racemic Mixture: A 50:50 mixture of d- and l-enantiomers. Optically inactive due to external compensation.

Stereochemical Outcomes

• **SN2 Stereochemistry:** Results in 100% **inversion of configuration** due to mandatory backside attack.

• **SN1 Stereochemistry:** Results in **racemisation** (partial or complete). Since intermediate carbocation is flat and planar, nucleophile has a 50% chance of attacking from either face, yielding a mixture of retention and inversion products.

Elimination Reactions & Metal Actions

Haloalkanes

Elimination (dehydrohalogenation)

Heating haloalkane with **alcoholic KOH** removes hydrogen from \(\beta\)-carbon and halogen from \(\alpha\)-carbon, forming an alkene.

• **Saytzeff / Zaitsev Rule:** In dehydrohalogenation reactions, the preferred product is that alkene which has the **greater number of alkyl groups** attached to the doubly bonded carbon atoms (more substituted/more stable alkene).

Reactions with Metals

• **Grignard Reagent:** Reacting with Mg in dry ether: \(R-X + Mg \rightarrow R-Mg-X\) (organometallic compound).

• **Wurtz Reaction:** Coupling of alkyl halides with sodium in dry ether to form symmetrical higher alkanes:

2R-X + 2Na ➔ R-R + 2NaX

• **Wurtz-Fittig:** Alkyl halide + aryl halide coupling. **Fittig:** Diaryl coupling.

Low Reactivity of Haloarenes

Haloalkanes

Aryl halides are extremely inert towards nucleophilic substitution compared to alkyl halides. Reasons:

Four Key Reasons

1. **Resonance Effect:** Lone pair on halogen delocalizes into the benzene ring, giving C-X bond a **partial double bond character**, making it hard to cleave.

2. **Hybridisation:** Carbon in aryl halide is \(sp^2\)-hybridized (more s-character, tighter hold of electrons, shorter bond) whereas alkyl is \(sp^3\).

3. **Instability of phenyl cation:** Cannot stabilize itself by resonance.

4. **Electron Repulsion:** Electron-rich benzene ring repels approaching nucleophiles.

Forcing Conditions

Substitution can occur under drastic conditions (Dow's process: chlorobenzene to phenol requires NaOH at 623 K, 300 atm).

The presence of electron-withdrawing groups (like -NO₂) at ortho- and para- positions greatly increases reactivity towards nucleophiles by stabilizing the intermediate carbanion.

Polyhalogen Compounds

Haloalkanes

Chloroform (CHCl₃)

Used as solvent. Stored in dark, closed bottles filled to the brim to prevent oxidation by air into highly poisonous gas **Phosgene (carbonyl chloride)**:

2CHCl₃ + O₂ ➔ 2COCl₂ + 2HCl

Freons & Iodoform

• **Freons (CFCs):** Stable, non-flammable refrigerants. Cause ozone layer depletion in stratosphere.

• **Iodoform (CHI₃):** Used as antiseptic. Antiseptic action is due to liberation of free iodine, not iodoform itself.

DDT & CCl₄

• **Carbon Tetrachloride:** Solvents. Damages liver, kidney, causes dizziness.

• **DDT (dichlorodiphenyltrichloroethane):** Non-biodegradable pesticide. Accumulates in food chain, disrupting calcium metabolism in birds.
