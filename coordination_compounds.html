Werner's Coordination Theory

Coordination Compounds

Alfred Werner proposed the first coherent theory of coordination compounds based on their chemical behaviour (e.g. reacting cobalt complexes with silver nitrate).

Postulates

1. **Primary Valence:** Ionizable valency. Corresponds to oxidation state of metal. Satisfied by negative ions.

2. **Secondary Valence:** Non-ionizable. Corresponds to **coordination number** (number of ligand bonds). Satisfied by neutral molecules or negative ions. It has directional characteristics, determining spatial geometry.

AgNO₃ Precipitation Proof

For 1 mole of cobalt complexes, moles of AgCl precipitate vary:

• CoCl₃·6NH₃ ➔ [Co(NH₃)₆]³⁺ 3Cl⁻ (3 AgCl)
• CoCl₃·5NH₃ ➔ [Co(NH₃)₅Cl]²⁺ 2Cl⁻ (2 AgCl)
• CoCl₃·4NH₃ ➔ [Co(NH₃)₄Cl₂]⁺ Cl⁻ (1 AgCl)

Coordination Terms & IUPAC Nomenclature

Coordination Compounds
  • Ligand: Ion or molecule that donates electron pair to central atom. (Unidentate, Didentate like oxalate/ethylenediamine, Polydentate like EDTA⁴⁻).
  • Ambidentate Ligand: Can coordinate through two different atoms (e.g. \(NO_2^-\) nitro vs \(ONO^-\) nitrito; \(SCN^-\) thiocyanato vs \(NCS^-\) isothiocyanato).
  • Homoleptic vs Heteroleptic: Homoleptic complexes bind only one type of ligand (e.g. \([Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}\)).

IUPAC Naming Order

1. Name cation first, then anion.

2. List ligands in alphabetical order with prefixes (di, tri, tetra, or bis, tris for complex ligands).

3. Name central metal. If complex is anionic, metal name ends in **-ate** (e.g., ferrate, cobaltate). Write oxidation state in Roman numerals in brackets.

[Co(NH₃)₄(H₂O)Cl]Cl₂ ➔ Tetraammineaquachloridocobalt(III) chloride

Structural Isomerism

Coordination Compounds

Linkage & Coordination Isomerism

• **Linkage Isomerism:** Occurs in complexes containing ambidentate ligands (e.g., \([Co(NH_3)_5(NO_2)]Cl_2\) - yellow, vs \([Co(NH_3)_5(ONO)]Cl_2\) - red).

• **Coordination Isomerism:** Interchange of ligands between cationic and anionic complex spheres:

[Co(NH₃)₆][Cr(CN)₆] & [Cr(NH₃)₆][Co(CN)₆]

Ionization & Solvate Isomerism

• **Ionization Isomerism:** Gives different ions in solution upon ionization:

[Co(NH₃)₅SO₄]Br (gives Br⁻) & [Co(NH₃)₅Br]SO₄ (gives SO₄²⁻)

• **Solvate / Hydrate Isomerism:** Differ in number of solvent water molecules acting directly as ligands vs free crystal waters:

[Cr(H₂O)₆]Cl₃ (violet) & [Cr(H₂O)₅Cl]Cl₂·H₂O (grey-green)

Stereoisomerism: Geometrical & Optical

Coordination Compounds

Geometrical Isomerism

• **Square Planar (\(MA_2B_2\)):** Shows cis- and trans- isomers (e.g. cisplatin vs transplatin).

• **Octahedral (\(MA_4B_2\)):** Shows cis- and trans- isomers.

• **Octahedral (\(MA_3B_3\)):** Shows **facial (fac)** and **meridional (mer)** isomers (e.g., \([Co(NH_3)_3(NO_2)_3]\)).

Optical Isomerism (Chiral Complexes)

Molecules that form non-superimposable mirror images. Rotate plane polarized light in opposite directions (dextro and laevo forms).

• Commonly shown by octahedral complexes containing didentate chelating ligands (e.g., \([Co(en)_3]^{3+}\)).

• Trans-isomers of type \([Pt(en)_2Cl_2]^{2+}\) have a plane of symmetry and are optically inactive (meso), while the cis-isomer is chiral and optically active.

Valence Bond Theory (VBT)

Coordination Compounds

Explains the hybridization and geometry of complexes based on orbital overlaps. For Octahedral coordination (CN=6):

  • Inner Orbital Complexes (\(d^2sp^3\)): Uses inner \((n-1)d\) orbitals for hybridisation. Occurs with strong field ligands that force pairing of electrons. Usually low spin/diamagnetic (e.g. \([Co(NH_3)_6]^{3+}\)).
  • Outer Orbital Complexes (\(sp^3d^2\)): Uses outer \(nd\) orbitals. Occurs with weak field ligands. High spin/paramagnetic (e.g. \([CoF_6]^{3-}\)).

Four-Coordination (CN=4)

• **\(sp^3\) Hybridisation:** Tetrahedral geometry (e.g. \([Ni(CO)_4]\), \([NiCl_4]^{2-}\)).

• **\(dsp^2\) Hybridisation:** Square planar geometry (e.g., \([Ni(CN)_4]^{2-}\)).

**VBT Limitation:** Cannot explain the color of complexes or quantify magnetic properties accurately, nor explain why some ligands are strong and others are weak.

Crystal Field Theory: Octahedral Splitting

Coordination Compounds

CFT treats ligands as **point negative charges** (or dipoles) and bonding as electrostatic. In an octahedral field, the six ligands approach along the x, y, and z axes.

This destabilizes orbitals lying directly on the axes (\(d_{x^2-y^2}\) and \(d_{z^2}\)) more than orbitals lying in between. Thus, degenerate d-orbitals split into:

  • **Lower energy \(t_{2g}\) triplet:** (\(d_{xy}, d_{yz}, d_{xz}\)) stabilized by \(-0.4 \Delta_o\).
  • **Higher energy \(e_g\) doublet:** stabilized by \(+0.6 \Delta_o\).

Strong vs Weak Fields

• **Strong Field Ligands:** Large \(\Delta_o\). If \(\Delta_o > P\) (pairing energy), electrons pair up in \(t_{2g}\) first, forming **low spin complexes**.

• **Weak Field Ligands:** Small \(\Delta_o\). If \(\Delta_o < P\), electrons remain unpaired and enter \(e_g\), forming **high spin complexes**.

**Spectrochemical Series:** Arrangement of ligands in increasing order of crystal field splitting strength: \(I^- < Br^- < Cl^- < F^- < OH^- < H_2O < NH_3 < en < CN^- < CO\).

CFT: Tetrahedral Splitting

Coordination Compounds

In tetrahedral coordination, the four ligands approach the central atom in between the axes.

Consequently, the \(d_{xy}, d_{yz}, d_{xz}\) orbitals experience more repulsion than the \(d_{x^2-y^2}\) and \(d_{z^2}\) orbitals. The splitting is reversed compared to octahedral:

  • **Lower energy \(e\) doublet:** stabilized by \(-0.6 \Delta_t\).
  • **Higher energy \(t_2\) triplet:** destabilized by \(+0.4 \Delta_t\).

Splitting Magnitude

Because there are fewer ligands (4 instead of 6) and they don't point directly at the orbitals, tetrahedral splitting is much smaller than octahedral:

Δ_t = (4/9) · Δ_o

Because \(\Delta_t\) is small, it rarely exceeds pairing energy (\(\Delta_t < P\)). Thus, **tetrahedral complexes are almost always high spin**.

Bonding in Metal Carbonyls

Coordination Compounds

Metal carbonyls are homoleptic complexes of transition metals with carbon monoxide (\(CO\)) as the ligand.

The metal-carbon bond possesses both **\(\sigma\) and \(\pi\) character**, creating a unique stabilizing interaction called **synergic bonding**:

  • **Sigma (\(\sigma\)) bond:** Formed by donation of lone pair of electrons from carbon of CO into vacant d-orbital of metal.
  • **Pi (\(\pi\)) bond:** Formed by back-donation of a pair of electrons from filled d-orbital of metal into vacant antibonding \(\pi^*\) orbital of CO.

Synergic Effect

This back-donation of charge from metal to ligand strengthens the ligand-to-metal donation bond, and vice-versa.

This mutual reinforcement (**synergic effect**) results in a highly stable, strong metal-carbon covalent bond.

Examples: \(Ni(CO)_4\) (tetrahedral), \(Fe(CO)_5\) (trigonal bipyramidal), \(Cr(CO)_6\) (octahedral).

Stability of Coordination Complexes

Coordination Compounds

Stability Constants

Refers to the degree of association between the metal ion and ligands in solution at equilibrium. For: \(M + 4L \rightleftharpoons ML_4\):

β_4 = [ML_4] / ([M] · [L]⁴)

\(\beta_4\) is the **overall stability constant**. A larger value represents a more stable complex.

Factors Affecting Stability

• **Charge & Size:** Smaller, highly charged metal ions form more stable complexes (higher charge density).

• **Basic Strength of Ligand:** More basic ligands donate electrons more easily, increasing stability.

• **Chelate Effect:** Chelating (ring-forming) ligands form much more stable complexes than unidentate ligands (due to positive entropy changes).
