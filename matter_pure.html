Is Matter Around Us Pure?

Pure substances, mixtures, solutions, colloids, suspensions, Tyndall effect, and separation techniques.

Pure Substances vs Mixtures

Pure Substances

Definite and fixed composition. Can be elements (cannot be split further: Fe, Au, O) or compounds (two or more elements in fixed ratio: H2O, NaCl, CO2).

Compound properties differ from constituent elements. E.g., Water (liquid) from Hydrogen (gas) + Oxygen (gas).

Mixtures

Components mixed in any proportion, separated by physical methods, properties are averages of components.

Homogeneous: Uniform composition (salt water, air, alloys like brass).
Heterogeneous: Visibly different components (soil, salad, granite).

Solutions, Colloids and Suspensions

  • True Solution: Particle size <1 nm. Homogeneous, stable, transparent, not filterable. Solute (dispersed phase) in Solvent (dispersion medium). Example: salt water, glucose in water, ethanol in water.
  • Colloidal Solution: Particle size 1-1000 nm. Appears homogeneous but is heterogeneous. Shows Tyndall Effect (scattering of light). Not filterable through paper. Example: milk, butter, fog, smoke, blood, ink, starch solution.
  • Suspension: Particle size >1000 nm. Heterogeneous, settles on standing (unstable), filterable. Example: chalk in water, muddy river water, sand in water.

Separation Techniques

Filtration

Removes insoluble solids from liquids using filter paper.

Evaporation

Removes dissolved solid by evaporating solvent. E.g., NaCl from seawater.

Centrifugation

Centrifugal force separates cream from milk, blood cells from plasma.

Distillation

Separates miscible liquids by different boiling points. E.g., water from acetone (b.p. 56C).

Magnetic Separation

Separates magnetic iron filings from non-magnetic sulphur using a magnet.

Chromatography

Separates dissolved coloured substances by differential adsorption. Ink separates into multiple dye components.

Physical and Chemical Changes

Physical Changes

  • No new substance formed
  • Reversible (mostly)
  • Only state, shape, or size changes

Examples: Melting ice, cutting paper, boiling water, dissolving sugar in water, tearing cloth.

Chemical Changes

  • New substance with different properties formed
  • Generally irreversible
  • Energy change (heat, light, sound) observed

Examples: Burning wood, rusting iron (4Fe + 3O2 + water gives Fe2O3.xH2O), cooking food, milk turning sour, photosynthesis.

Saturated vs Unsaturated Solutions

Understanding concentration limits is vital for separating mixtures:

  • Saturated Solution: No more solute can dissolve at that particular temperature.
  • Solubility: The maximum mass of solute that dissolves in 100g of solvent at a specific temperature.
  • Suspension: Heterogeneous mixture with large particles (> 1000 nm) that settle down.

NCERT Solved Numerical: Solution Concentration

Formula: Mass percentage of solution $$\text{Mass } \% = \frac{\text{Mass of Solute}}{\text{Mass of Solution}} \times 100$$ $$\text{Mass of Solution} = \text{Mass of Solute} + \text{Mass of Solvent}$$

Problem: A solution contains 40g of common salt dissolved in 320g of water. Calculate the concentration of the solution.

Step-by-step Calculation: $$\text{Mass of solute} = 40\text{g},\ \text{Mass of solvent (water)} = 320\text{g}$$ $$\text{Mass of solution} = 40 + 320 = 360\text{g}$$ $$\text{Mass } \% = \frac{40}{360} \times 100 = 11.11\%$$
