Some Basic Concepts of Chemistry

Focusing on laws of chemical combinations, mole concept, stoichiometry, and empirical formulas.

Learning Objectives

  • Understand the laws of chemical combinations.
  • Master the concept of the Mole and Avogadro's Number.
  • Calculate empirical and molecular formulas from chemical percentages.
  • Perform stoichiometry and limiting reactant calculations.

Laws of Chemical Combinations

1. Law of Conservation of Mass

Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction (Lavoisier, 1789).

$$\sum \text{Mass of Reactants} = \sum \text{Mass of Products}$$

2. Law of Definite Proportions

A chemical compound always contains exactly the same proportion of elements by mass (Proust, 1799).

Law of Multiple Proportions

If two elements combine to form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fixed mass of the other are in the ratio of small whole numbers (Dalton, 1803).

Example: Carbon and Oxygen form $CO$ and $CO_2$. For 12g Carbon, Oxygen masses are 16g and 32g. Ratio: $$16:32 = 1:2$$

The Mole Concept

One mole is the amount of substance containing as many entities (atoms, molecules, ions) as there are atoms in exactly 12g of Carbon-12.

$$N_A = 6.022 \times 10^{23} \text{ entities/mol}$$

Moles from Mass: $$n = \frac{\text{Given Mass (g)}}{\text{Molar Mass (g/mol)}}$$
Moles from Gas Volume (STP): $$n = \frac{\text{Volume (L)}}{22.4 \text{ L}}$$

Empirical vs Molecular Formulas

Empirical Formula: Represents the simplest whole-number ratio of various atoms present in a compound.

Molecular Formula: Represents the exact number of various types of atoms present in a molecule of a compound.

$$\text{Molecular Formula} = n \times \text{Empirical Formula}$$

$$n = \frac{\text{Molar Mass}}{\text{Empirical Formula Mass}}$$

Stoichiometry and Calculations

Stoichiometry deals with calculation of masses (or volumes) of reactants and products involved in chemical reactions.

Limiting Reagent: The reactant which is completely consumed first in a reaction, limiting the amount of product formed.

Solution Concentrations: Molarity vs Molality

Concentration terms describe solute-solvent ratios in chemical reactions:

  • Molarity ($M$): Moles of solute per litre of solution. Temp dependent. $$M = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{V_{\text{solution (L)}}}$$
  • Molality ($m$): Moles of solute per kilogram of solvent. Temp independent. $$m = \frac{n_{\text{solute}}}{\text{Mass of solvent (kg)}}$$

NCERT Solved Numerical: Molarity Math

Problem: Calculate the molarity of NaOH in a solution prepared by dissolving 4g of NaOH in enough water to form 250 mL of solution.

Calculation: * Molar mass of NaOH = $23 + 16 + 1 = 40\text{ g/mol}$. * Moles of NaOH = $4\text{g} / 40\text{ g/mol} = 0.1\text{ mol}$. * Volume of solution = $250\text{ mL} = 0.25\text{ L}$. * Molarity = $0.1\text{ mol} / 0.25\text{ L} = 0.40\text{ M}$.
