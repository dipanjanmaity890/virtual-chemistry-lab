Equilibrium

Dynamic nature of equilibrium, Le Chatelier's principle, ionic equilibrium, pH scale, Henderson-Hasselbalch equation.

Le Chatelier's Principle

If a system at equilibrium is subjected to a change in concentration, temperature, or pressure, the system shifts to counteract the imposed change.

Haber Process: $$N_2(g) + 3H_2(g) \rightleftharpoons 2NH_3(g) \quad (\Delta H = -92 \text{ kJ/mol})$$ • **High Pressure**: shifts to right (fewer gaseous moles). • **Low Temperature**: shifts to right (exothermic).

Ionic Equilibrium & pH

Water auto-ionization constant:

$$K_w = [H^+][OH^-] = 1.0 \times 10^{-14} \quad (\text{at } 298\text{ K})$$

$$pH = -\log[H^+]$$

Buffer Solutions: Resist changes in pH upon addition of small amounts of acid/base. • **Acidic Buffer**: Weak acid + conjugate salt. • **Basic Buffer**: Weak base + conjugate salt.

Henderson-Hasselbalch Equation

Used to calculate pH of buffer solutions:

$$pH = pK_a + \log \left( \frac{[\text{Conjugate Base}]}{[\text{Acid}]} \right)$$

When $[\text{Base}] = [\text{Acid}]$, $pH = pK_a$. This forms the maximum buffering capacity.
