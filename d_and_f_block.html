Definition of Transition Elements

d- and f-Block

The d-block contains elements of Groups 3-12, characterized by the filling of d-orbitals in their penultimate shells.

Definition & Scope

A **transition element** is defined as one which has **partially filled d-orbitals** in its ground state or in any of its common oxidation states.

General Electronic Configuration:

(n-1)d^(1-10) ns^(1-2)

The Group 12 Exception

â€¢ **Zinc (Zn), Cadmium (Cd), and Mercury (Hg)** have completely filled d-subshells (\(d^{10}\)) in both their ground state and common oxidation states (e.g. \(Zn^{2+}\): \(3d^{10} 4s^0\)).

Hence, they are d-block elements, but not classified as transition elements.

Variable Oxidation States

d- and f-Block

Unlike s-block elements, transition metals exhibit **multiple oxidation states** in their compounds because both \((n-1)d\) and \(ns\) electrons can participate in chemical bonding (due to very small energy differences).

  • **Manganese (Mn)** exhibits maximum number of oxidation states (+2 to +7) in the 3d series.
  • **Scandium (Sc)** shows only +3 (not variable, since \(Sc^{3+}\) has noble gas config).
  • **Zinc (Zn)** shows only +2 (non-variable).

Stability Rules

â€¢ Oxidation states corresponding to noble gas, half-filled (\(d^5\)), or fully-filled (\(d^{10}\)) configurations are more stable.

â€¢ For transition elements, higher oxidation states are more stable down a group (e.g. \(W(VI)\) in \(WO_4^{2-}\) is highly stable, whereas \(Cr(VI)\) in \(CrO_4^{2-}\) is a strong oxidizing agent).

d- and f-Block

Magnetic Properties

â€¢ **Diamagnetic:** All electrons are paired; weakly repelled by magnetic field.

â€¢ **Paramagnetic:** Unpaired electrons are present; attracted by magnetic field. Spin-only magnetic moment (\(\mu\)) calculated in Bohr Magnetons (B.M.):

Catalytic Activity

Transition metals and their compounds are highly effective catalysts (e.g. \(V_2O_5\) in Contact Process, finely divided \(Fe\) in Haber's Process). Reasons:

1. Ability to adopt **multiple oxidation states** to form unstable intermediate complexes.
2. Large surface area provides active sites for adsorption, lowering activation energy.

Color & Alloy Formation

d- and f-Block

d-d Transition (Color)

Most transition metal complexes are colored. Under the influence of ligands, the degenerate d-orbitals split into t2g and eg groups.

â€¢ Absorption of white light excites an electron from lower d-orbital to higher d-orbital (**d-d transition**).

â€¢ Transmitted light is the complementary color. Note: \(Sc^{3+}\) (\(d^0\)) and \(Zn^{2+}\) (\(d^{10}\)) are colorless.

Interstitial Compounds & Alloys

â€¢ **Interstitial Compounds:** Formed when small atoms (H, C, N) get trapped in crystal lattices. They are hard, chemically inert, and have higher melting points than pure metals.

â€¢ **Alloys:** Solid solutions formed by mixing metals whose atomic sizes differ by <15% (Hume-Rothery rule). (e.g. Steel, Brass).

f-Block: Lanthanoid Contraction

d- and f-Block

Lanthanoid Contraction: The steady, gradual decrease in atomic and ionic radii of lanthanoids (\(Ln^{3+}\) ions) with increasing atomic number from Lanthanum (57) to Lutetium (71).

Cause: Poor shielding effect of 4f electrons. As nuclear charge increases by 1 at each step, the 4f electrons shield the outer shell poorly, pulling electrons closer to the nucleus.

Key Consequences

1. **Post-Lanthanoid Radii:** 4d and 5d transition elements have identical sizes (e.g. Zr=160pm, Hf=159pm). They occur together in nature, making separation difficult.

2. **Basicity Decrease:** Size decreases, increasing covalent character of metal-hydroxide bonds (Fajans' rule). Thus, \(La(OH)_3\) is most basic; \(Lu(OH)_3\) is least basic.
