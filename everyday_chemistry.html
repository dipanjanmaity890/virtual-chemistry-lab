Classification of Drugs

Everyday Chemistry

Drugs: Low molecular mass chemicals that interact with macromolecular targets to produce a biological response. Classified by:

  • Pharmacological Effect: Useful for doctors (e.g. analgesics reduce pain, antiseptics kill microbes).
  • Drug Action: Based on action on biochemical process (e.g. antihistamines block histamine receptor).
  • Chemical Structure: Common structural features (e.g., sulfonamides).

Molecular Targets

Based on target interaction (enzymes, receptors, nucleic acids).

• **Enzyme Inhibitors:** block active site or bind allosteric site to change enzyme shape.

• **Receptor Antagonists:** bind to receptor site and inhibit natural message transmission.

Therapeutic Action: Key Classes

Everyday Chemistry

Antacids

Relieve acidity by neutralizing HCl in stomach. Modern drugs block histamine receptors that trigger acid secretion (e.g. Cimetidine, Ranitidine).

Antihistamines

Block the inflammatory transmitter histamine. Treat allergies (e.g. Brompheniramine, Terfenadine).

Tranquilizers

Neurologically active drugs. Reduce stress, anxiety, or mental illness (e.g., Equanil, Valium, Barbiturates).

Analgesics & Antimicrobials

Everyday Chemistry

Analgesics (Pain Relievers)

• Non-Narcotic: Non-addictive. Inhibit prostaglandin synthesis (e.g., Aspirin, Paracetamol). Aspirin also acts as anti-blood clotting agent.

• Narcotic: Addictive morphine derivatives. Relieve severe pain, induce sleep (e.g. Heroin, Codeine).

Antimicrobials

• Antibiotics: Kill/inhibit microbes (e.g., Penicillin - narrow spectrum; Chloramphenicol - broad spectrum).

• Antiseptics: Applied to living tissue (e.g. Dettol - chloroxylenol + terpineol, Bithional in soaps).

• Disinfectants: Applied to inanimate objects (e.g. 1% Phenol).

Chemicals in Food

Everyday Chemistry

Chemicals are added to food for preservation, appearance, and sweetness.

  • Food Preservatives: Prevent spoilage due to microbial growth (e.g., Sodium benzoate, table salt, vegetable oils).
  • Antioxidants: Prevent oxidation of fats/oils (e.g. BHA - Butylated hydroxyanisole, BHT).

Artificial Sweetening Agents

Provide sweetness without caloric intake:

• Aspartame: 100 times sweeter than sucrose. Unstable at cooking temp, used in cold foods/drinks.

• Alitame: High potency sweetener (2000 times sweeter). Control of sweetness level is difficult.

• Sucralose: Trichloro derivative of sucrose. Stable at cooking temperature.

Cleansing Agents: Soaps

Everyday Chemistry

Soaps are sodium or potassium salts of long-chain fatty acids (stearic, palmitic, oleic acids).

Saponification: Prepared by heating fat/oil (glyceryl ester of fatty acid) with aqueous sodium hydroxide.

Glyceryl ester + 3NaOH ➔ Soap + Glycerol

Hard Water Limitation

Soaps **do not work in hard water** containing calcium and magnesium salts. They react to form insoluble sticky precipitates (scum):

2C₁₇H₃₅COONa + Ca²⁺ ➔ (C₁₇H₃₅COO)₂Ca(s) + 2Na⁺

This scum sticks to fabric fibers, reducing cleansing power.

Synthetic Detergents

Everyday Chemistry

Cleansing agents which have all the properties of soap but **work in both soft and hard water** because their calcium and magnesium salts are soluble in water.

Anionic

Sodium salts of sulfonated long-chain alcohols. Large anionic active part (e.g. Sodium lauryl sulfate). Used in toothpastes, household washing.

Cationic

Quaternary ammonium salts with chlorides/bromides. Cationic active part (e.g. Cetyltrimethylammonium bromide). Have germicidal properties; used in hair conditioners.

Non-ionic

Do not contain any ions. Formed when stearic acid reacts with polyethylene glycol. Used in liquid dishwashing detergents.

Cleansing Action & Micelle Formation

Everyday Chemistry

A soap molecule (e.g., sodium stearate: C₁₇H₃₅COONa) consists of:

  • Hydrophobic Tail: Long hydrocarbon chain (C₁₇H₃₅-), wetted by grease/oil, repelled by water.
  • Hydrophilic Head: Polar carboxylate group (-COO⁻ Na⁺), wetted by water.

Micelle Mechanism

Around grease/dirt, soap molecules arrange radially: hydrophobic tails insert into the grease droplet, and hydrophilic heads point outwards into water.

Upon agitation, the wetted heads pull the grease droplet off the fabric surface and suspend it in water as a stable emulsion.
