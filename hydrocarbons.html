Hydrocarbons

Alkanes, alkenes, alkynes, Markovnikov addition rule, ozonolysis double-bond cleavage, and benzene electrophilic substitution.

Electrophilic Addition to Alkenes

Alkenes contain carbon-carbon double bonds, acting as rich sources of $\pi$ electrons that readily undergo electrophilic additions.

Markovnikov's Rule: During addition of unsymmetrical reagent (e.g., $H-Br$) to unsymmetrical alkene (e.g., propene), the positive part of the reagent ($H^+$) adds to the carbon having more hydrogen atoms.

This is guided by the stability of the intermediate carbocation: **$2^\circ$ carbocation (more stable) is formed preferentially over $1^\circ$ carbocation.**

Alkene Ozonolysis Cleavage

Reaction of alkenes with ozone ($O_3$) to form an ozonide intermediate, followed by reductive cleavage with $Zn/H_2O$ to yield aldehydes or ketones.

Ozonolysis Mechanism: $$R-CH=CH-R \xrightarrow{O_3} \text{Ozonide Ring} \xrightarrow{Zn/H_2O} R-CHO + R-CHO$$ This reaction is highly valuable to locate positions of double bonds in unknown alkenes.

Aromaticity & Electrophilic Substitution

Benzene exhibits high resonance stability (aromaticity), favoring electrophilic aromatic substitution ($S_EAr$) over addition:

  • 1. Halogenation: Substitution of $H$ by $Cl$ using $Cl_2 + FeCl_3$ catalyst.
  • 2. Nitration: Using conc. $HNO_3$ + conc. $H_2SO_4$ to insert nitronium ion ($NO_2^+$).
  • 3. Friedel-Crafts Alkylation: Using alkyl chloride ($R-Cl$) + $AlCl_3$ to yield alkylbenzene.
