Carbonyl Group Structure & Polarity

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

The carbonyl group (\(C=O\)) consists of a double bond between carbon and oxygen, representing the core functional center of aldehydes, ketones, and carboxylic acids.

Bond Geometry

• The carbonyl carbon is **\(sp^2\)-hybridized** and forms three \(\sigma\) bonds lying in one plane at angles of approx **120°** (trigonal planar).

• The fourth valence electron of carbon occupies a unhybridized p-orbital, overlapping laterally with a p-orbital of oxygen to form a \(\pi\) bond above and below the \(\sigma\) plane.

High Polarization

• Oxygen is much more electronegative than carbon, pulling the \(\pi\) electron cloud towards itself.

• The C=O bond is **highly polar**, with carbon carrying partial positive charge (\(\delta^+\)) acting as an **electrophile**, and oxygen carrying partial negative charge (\(\delta^-\)) acting as a **nucleophile**.

Preparation of Aldehydes & Ketones

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

1. Common Methods

• **Oxidation:** \(1^\circ\) alcohols ➔ Aldehydes (using PCC); \(2^\circ\) alcohols ➔ Ketones (using \(CrO_3\)).

• **Ozonolysis of Alkenes:** Cleaving C=C bonds with \(O_3\) followed by \(Zn/H_2O\) yields carbonyl fragments.

• **Hydration of Alkynes:** Adding \(H_2O\) in presence of \(H_2SO_4\)/\(HgSO_4\). Ethyne yields acetaldehyde; higher alkynes yield ketones.

2. Named Preparations

• **Rosenmund Reduction (Aldehydes):** Catalytic hydrogenation of acid chloride over \(Pd-BaSO_4\) poisoned with sulfur:

R-COCl + H₂ ➔ [Pd-BaSO₄] ➔ R-CHO + HCl

• **Stephen Reaction:** Reduction of nitriles with \(SnCl_2/HCl\) followed by hydrolysis.

• **Etard Reaction:** Chromyl chloride (\(CrO_2Cl_2\)) oxidizes toluene to benzaldehyde.

Nucleophilic Addition Mechanism & Reactivity

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

General Mechanism: A nucleophile attacks the electrophilic carbonyl carbon from a direction perpendicular to the plane of \(sp^2\) orbitals, converting the planar carbon to a tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate. Acid protonation then yields the final addition product.

Nu⁻ + R₂C=O ➔ R₂C(Nu)-O⁻ ➔ [H⁺] ➔ R₂C(Nu)-OH

Reactivity: Aldehyde vs Ketone

Aldehydes are more reactive than ketones towards nucleophilic attack. Reasons:

1. **Steric Hindrance:** Ketones have two bulky alkyl groups shielding the carbon, hindering nucleophilic approach compared to one group in aldehydes.

2. **Inductive Effect:** Alkyl groups are electron-donating (+I effect). Two alkyl groups in ketones reduce the partial positive charge on the carbonyl carbon, making it less electrophilic.

Key Carbonyl Addition Reactions

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

HCN & NaHSO₃

• **HCN addition:** Yields **cyanohydrins** (essential intermediates for \(\alpha\)-hydroxy acids).

• **Bisulfite addition:** Forms crystalline bisulfite adducts. Used for separation and purification of carbonyl compounds.

Alcohols & Grignards

• **Alcohols:** Acid-catalyzed reaction yields hemiacetals/hemiketals and then stable **acetals/ketals** (used as protecting groups for carbonyls).

• **Grignards:** Yields primary, secondary, or tertiary alcohols.

Ammonia Derivatives

Reactions with \(H_2N-Z\) eliminate water to form \(C=N-Z\) linkages:
• Hydroxylamine ➔ Oximes.
• Hydrazine ➔ Hydrazones.
• **2,4-DNP:** Yields bright yellow, orange, or red crystalline precipitates (used to identify carbonyls).

Oxidation Tests & Reduction Pathways

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

Distinction: Tollens' & Fehling's

Aldehydes oxidize easily; ketones do not.

• **Tollens' Test (Silver Mirror):** Aldehydes reduce ammoniacal silver nitrate to metallic silver, forming a **silver mirror** coating on the tube:

R-CHO + 2[Ag(NH₃)₂]⁺ + 3OH⁻ ➔ R-COO⁻ + 2Ag↓ + 4NH₃ + 2H₂O

• **Fehling's Test:** Aldehydes reduce alkaline Cu²⁺ tartrate complexes, forming a **red cuprous oxide (\(Cu_2O\)) precipitate**.

Reduction to Hydrocarbons

• **Clemmensen Reduction:** Carbonyls reduced to \(CH_2\) groups using **amalgamated zinc and conc. HCl**:

C=O + 4[H] ➔ [Zn-Hg / HCl] ➔ CH₂ + H₂O

• **Wolff-Kishner Reduction:** Carbonyls heated with hydrazine (\(H_2N-NH_2\)) and KOH in ethylene glycol to yield alkanes.

Aldol Condensation

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

Aldehydes and ketones possessing **at least one \(\alpha\)-hydrogen** undergo a reaction in the presence of dilute alkali to form \(\beta\)-hydroxy aldehydes (aldols) or \(\beta\)-hydroxy ketones (ketols), which eliminate water upon heating to yield \(\alpha,\beta\)-unsaturated carbonyl compounds.

2CH₃-CHO ➔ [dil. NaOH] ➔ CH₃-CH(OH)-CH₂-CHO ➔ [heat, -H₂O] ➔ CH₃-CH=CH-CHO (But-2-enal)

Cross-Aldol Condensation

• Occurs between two different carbonyl molecules.

• If both reactants contain \(\alpha\)-hydrogens, a mixture of **four different products** is formed, which is synthetically less useful.

• If only one reactant has an \(\alpha\)-hydrogen (e.g. Benzaldehyde + Acetaldehyde), a single major cross-product forms easily (Claisen-Schmidt reaction).

Cannizzaro Reaction

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

Aldehydes **lacking an \(\alpha\)-hydrogen** (e.g. Formaldehyde \(HCHO\), Benzaldehyde \(C_6H_5CHO\)) do not undergo Aldol condensation.

Instead, when treated with **concentrated alkali (50% NaOH/KOH)**, they undergo self-oxidation-reduction (**disproportionation**):

  • One molecule is reduced to an **alcohol**.
  • The other molecule is oxidized to a **carboxylic acid salt**.

Equation & Mechanism

2HCHO + conc. NaOH ➔ CH₃OH + HCOONa

**Hydride Transfer:** Hydroxide attacks one carbonyl carbon, yielding a tetrahedral intermediate. This intermediate transfers a **hydride ion (\(H^-\))** directly to the second carbonyl molecule (the rate-determining step), resulting in simultaneous oxidation and reduction.

Preparation of Carboxylic Acids

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

Oxidative Methods

• **From Alcohols/Aldehydes:** Oxidation using powerful reagents like chromic acid (\(H_2CrO_4\)) or alkaline \(KMnO_4\).

• **From Alkylbenzenes:** Regardless of side chain length, heating with alkaline \(KMnO_4\) oxidizes the chain to a carboxyl group, yielding **Benzoic Acid**.

Hydrolysis Pathways

• **From Nitriles / Amides:** Nitriles hydrolyze in acidic or basic medium first to amides, then to carboxylic acids:

R-CN ➔ [+H₂O/H⁺] ➔ R-CONH₂ ➔ R-COOH

• **From Esters / Anhydrides:** Acidic or basic hydrolysis splits esters into carboxylic acids and alcohols.

Grignard Carbonation

Reacting Grignard reagents (\(RMgX\)) with **dry ice (solid \(CO_2\))** in dry ether forms a carboxylate salt intermediate, which hydrolyzes to a carboxylic acid:

R-MgX + CO₂ ➔ R-COOMgX ➔ [H₂O/H⁺] ➔ R-COOH

Carboxylic Acid Acidity & Reactions

Aldehydes, Ketones & Carboxylic Acids

Resonance Acidity

• Carboxylic acids are stronger acids than phenols because the negative charge in the **carboxylate ion** is delocalized over **two highly electronegative oxygen atoms**, whereas in phenoxide it is shared by less electronegative carbon atoms in the ring.

• Electron-withdrawing substituents increase acidity by dispersing negative charge (e.g., \(CF_3COOH > CCl_3COOH > CHCl_2COOH > CH_2ClCOOH > CH_3COOH\)).

HVZ & Decarboxylation

• **Hell-Volhard-Zelinsky (HVZ) Reaction:** Carboxylic acids having \(\alpha\)-hydrogen react with \(Cl_2\) or \(Br_2\) in presence of red phosphorus to yield **\(\alpha\)-halo carboxylic acids**:

R-CH₂-COOH ➔ [X₂ / Red P] ➔ R-CH(X)-COOH

• **Decarboxylation:** Sodium salts of carboxylic acids heated with **sodalime (NaOH + CaO in 3:1 ratio)** lose carbon dioxide to yield alkanes.
