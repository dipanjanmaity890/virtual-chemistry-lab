Carbon and Its Compounds

Tetravalency, catenation, allotropes, homologous series, functional groups, and important organic compounds.

Unique Properties of Carbon

  • Tetravalency: Carbon has 4 valence electrons and can form 4 covalent bonds. Can bond with H, O, N, S, halogens, and other C atoms. This allows formation of countless organic molecules.
  • Catenation: Carbon atoms bond to each other forming chains (straight and branched), rings, and complex 3D structures. No other element shows catenation to this extent.
  • Multiple Bonds: Forms single (C-C, 347 kJ/mol), double (C=C, 614 kJ/mol), and triple (C-C-C triple bond, 839 kJ/mol) bonds with other carbons.
  • Allotropes of Carbon: Diamond (sp3, tetrahedral, hardest known substance, 3.5 g/cm3), Graphite (sp2, layered sheets, electrical conductor, lubricant), Fullerene C60 (soccer-ball structure, superconductor, drug delivery).

Homologous Series and Functional Groups

A homologous series is a group of organic compounds with the same functional group and general formula. Consecutive members differ by -CH2- (14 u) in molecular mass.

Key Functional Groups

  • Alcohol: -OH (Ethanol C2H5OH)
  • Aldehyde: -CHO (Methanal, Ethanal)
  • Ketone: -CO- (Propanone, nail remover)
  • Carboxylic Acid: -COOH (Ethanoic Acid, vinegar)
  • Ester: -COO- (fruity aroma, used in perfumes)

Alkane Series (CnH2n+2)

  • CH4: Methane (natural gas)
  • C2H6: Ethane
  • C3H8: Propane (LPG)
  • C4H10: Butane (lighter fuel)

Alkenes (CnH2n): C2H4 Ethene, C3H6 Propene
Alkynes (CnH2n-2): C2H2 Ethyne (acetylene)

Chemical Properties

  • Combustion: CH4+2O2 gives CO2+2H2O+Heat. Complete combustion = blue flame + CO2+H2O. Incomplete combustion = yellow sooty flame + CO (toxic) + C (soot).
  • Oxidation of Alcohols: CH3CH2OH gives CH3COOH (Ethanol oxidised to Ethanoic Acid using alkaline KMnO4 or acidified K2Cr2O7, which acts as oxidising agent).
  • Addition Reaction: CH2=CH2 + H2 gives CH3-CH3 (Ni catalyst). CH2=CH2 + Cl2 gives CH2Cl-CH2Cl. Unsaturated compounds add across double/triple bond.
  • Substitution Reaction: CH4+Cl2 gives CH3Cl+HCl (in sunlight). Saturated hydrocarbons undergo substitution (not addition).
  • Esterification: CH3COOH + C2H5OH gives CH3COOC2H5 + H2O (conc. H2SO4 catalyst). Reverse (hydrolysis) in saponification makes soap.

Soaps, Detergents and Important Compounds

Soap and Micelle Action

Soap = sodium/potassium salt of long-chain fatty acid (e.g., sodium stearate C17H35COONa). Has hydrophilic head (-COO-Na+, water-loving) and hydrophobic tail (long carbon chain, oil-loving).

In water, soap forms micelles around grease: tails point inward (into grease), heads point outward (into water). Micelle is washed away with water. Soaps do NOT work in hard water.

Ethanol and Ethanoic Acid

Ethanol (C2H5OH): Made by fermentation of glucose (yeast, 37C): C6H12O6 gives 2C2H5OH + 2CO2. Used in beverages, medicines (tincture iodine), fuel (20% with petrol = gasohol).

Ethanoic Acid (CH3COOH): Main component of vinegar (3-4%). MP = 16.6C (glacial acetic acid). Used in making esters, acetate rayon, dyes, plastics.

Versatile Nature of Carbon

Carbon forms a huge number of compounds due to two unique properties:

  • Catenation: The ability of carbon atoms to form long covalent chains with other carbon atoms.
  • Tetravalency: Having 4 valence electrons, carbon can bond with oxygen, hydrogen, nitrogen, etc.
  • Isomerism: Same molecular formula but different structural configurations.

NCERT Solved Numerical: Hydrocarbon Isomers

Problem: Draw the carbon skeletons and name the isomers of Pentane ($C_5H_{12}$).

The 3 Isomers of Pentane: 1. **n-Pentane:** $CH_3-CH_2-CH_2-CH_2-CH_3$ (straight chain) 2. **Isopentane (2-methylbutane):** Branch at Carbon-2. 3. **Neopentane (2,2-dimethylpropane):** Two branches at Carbon-2.
